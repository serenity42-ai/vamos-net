# Brussels P2 2026: Two Statement Wins for the New Pairs

Brussels P2 2026 ended with two of the year's biggest upsets. Juan Lebrón and Leo Augsburger beat Arturo Coello and Agustín Tapia 2-6, 6-3, 6-3 in the men's final. Beatriz González and Paula Josemaría beat Gemma Triay and Delfina Brea 7-5, 6-2 in the women's. Both winning pairs were formed in the off-season. Both took down the world No. 1s in their first Premier Padel final together. The 2026 race opened up over a single Sunday afternoon on Court Cbc.

The men's final was the upset of the season so far. The Golden Boys came in as the world No. 1 pair and took the first set 6-2 in their familiar manner: ruthless. Then the match flipped. Lebrón and Augsburger took the second set 6-3 and the third by the same score, chasing down the world's most dominant duo over an hour and thirty-seven minutes. It was the first title for El Lobo since his partnership with Alejandro Galán ended, and the redemption arc the off-season storylines had been waiting for. Augsburger's role mattered. The 24-year-old Argentine has been seen as the partnership's wild card, a young winger whose explosive play needed structure. In Brussels he provided it.

Behind the final, the men's draw delivered the chaos that defines a P2. Franco Stupaczuk and Miguel Yanguas, another off-season pair, demolished Pablo Bautista and Lucas Campagnolo 6-1, 6-2 in the quarters before falling 5-7, 2-6 to the Golden Boys in the semis. Chingalán reached the same stage with a 6-1, 6-0 sweep of Paquito Navarro and Fran Guerrero, then found Lebrón and Augsburger's net pressure too much and lost 4-6, 4-6. The biggest scare for the Golden Boys came earlier, in a tight 7-6(4), 5-7, 6-3 quarterfinal against Coki Nieto and Jon Sanz. Two finished sets at 5-7 and 4-6 against the world's top pair was a result that didn't happen often in 2025.

The women's final was, if anything, more emphatic. González and Josemaría have been one of the women's tour's biggest stories since they paired up in the off-season after Josemaría parted ways with Ariana Sánchez, ending five years of dominance. In Brussels they delivered. Triay and Brea took an early break in the first set, but Perlamita responded by closing the set 7-5 and running away with the second 6-2. The semifinal route had been more dramatic: González and Josemaría came from a 3-6 first-set down to beat Sofía Araújo and Ariana Sánchez 6-3, 6-0, the heaviest scoreline of the tournament's late rounds.

Brussels was the first European stop of the season, and the implications travel well past one trophy. The Golden Boys remain the team to beat, but they are no longer untouchable. The two new partnerships at the top of both draws have made the 2026 race genuinely open in a way few were projecting six weeks ago. The tour now heads to Rome for the year's first Major. Tapia and Brea will want answers fast.
