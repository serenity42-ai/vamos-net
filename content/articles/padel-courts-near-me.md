# How to Find Padel Courts Near You: The Complete Guide

A decade ago, finding a padel court outside Spain or Argentina was a real expedition. In 2026, courts are popping up from London to Dubai to Tokyo, and the practical challenge is no longer existence; it's discovery. Here are the apps, platforms, and search techniques that work.

## The booking platforms

### Playtomic — the global default

Playtomic is the largest padel booking platform in the world, operating in over 40 countries with thousands of connected clubs. If there's a padel court near you, Playtomic almost certainly has it.

What it does:

- Court search and booking with real-time availability
- Player matching, for when you don't have a group of four
- Club profiles with photos, reviews, court types, and pricing
- In-app payment so you can book and pay in seconds
- Player rankings and stats for competitive players
- Tournament and league listings

How to use it:

1. Download the Playtomic app (iOS or Android) or visit playtomic.io
2. Set your location or search for a specific city
3. Browse available courts, filtered by date, time, and price
4. Book and pay through the app
5. Show up and play

Playtomic charges clubs a commission on bookings made through the platform. For players, the price shown in the app is the full price; no extra fees.

**Tip:** turn on notifications for your favourite clubs. Popular courts at peak times (weekday evenings, weekend mornings) book up fast, often days in advance.

### Padel Manager — Southern Europe and Latin America

Padel Manager is a club-management system that also offers a player-facing booking interface. Particularly popular in Spain and Portugal.

It handles:

- Court booking for clubs that use the system
- Class and coaching session bookings
- Club membership management
- Tournament registration

Padel Manager is less of a discovery platform than Playtomic. It works best when you already know which club you want to book at and they're on the system.

### Matchi — the Nordic specialist

Matchi is widely used in Sweden, Finland, Norway, and Denmark, where padel has had explosive growth. If you're looking for courts in Scandinavia, Matchi is often the primary booking platform.

### Other regional platforms

Depending on location:

- **Racketpal** (UK and Ireland)
- **Padeltrack** (various European markets)
- **Individual club websites and apps** (many larger clubs run their own booking systems)

## Using Google Maps

A surprisingly effective tool, especially in areas where dedicated padel apps have limited coverage.

### How to search

1. Open Google Maps (app or web)
2. Search for **"padel court"**, **"padel club"**, or **"padel near me"**
3. Scan results for locations, ratings, and reviews
4. Tap on a result to see photos, hours, contact information, and reviews

### Tips for better results

- **Try multiple search terms.** Some facilities list themselves as "padel center," "padel club," or even "sports center with padel." Try variations if the first search comes up empty.
- **Check the reviews.** Google reviews from other players often mention court quality, surface type, lighting, and availability — information that official listings rarely include.
- **Look at the photos.** User-uploaded photos give you a realistic picture of the facility. Check the condition of the glass walls, surface quality, and overall cleanliness.
- **Verify hours.** Some courts have limited hours or seasonal closures. Call ahead or check the website if you're visiting for the first time.
- **Use satellite view.** Padel courts are easy to spot from above: distinctive rectangular enclosed structures, often with blue or green surfaces visible.

## Federation directories

### National federations

Most national padel or tennis federations maintain directories of registered courts and clubs:

- **Spain:** Real Federación Española de Padel (FEP)
- **Italy:** Federazione Italiana Tennis e Padel (FITP)
- **France:** Fédération Française de Tennis (FFT) — padel falls under the tennis federation
- **UK:** Lawn Tennis Association (LTA) — includes a padel court finder
- **Sweden:** Svenska Padelförbundet
- **Germany:** Deutscher Padel Bund

These directories are often the most complete listings for their countries, including courts that don't appear on commercial booking platforms.

### International Padel Federation

The FIP website (padelfip.com) provides links to national federations worldwide, which can route you to local court directories.

## What kind of court is it?

Not every padel court is the same.

### Indoor

- Climate-controlled environments
- Consistent year-round
- Higher booking rates
- Common in Northern Europe, the UK, and regions with variable weather
- Typically artificial grass or carpet surfaces

### Outdoor

- Natural lighting during daytime
- Often with floodlights for evening sessions
- Subject to weather (rain, wind, extreme heat)
- Generally lower booking rates
- Common in Spain, Portugal, southern France, and the Middle East (during cooler months)

### Glass courts vs concrete-wall courts

- **Glass courts** provide the standard playing experience: predictable ball rebounds and spectator visibility
- **Concrete or brick wall courts** are cheaper to build and maintain but offer a different (faster) rebound
- Professional and most modern facilities use glass

### Surface types

- **Artificial grass with sand infill** (blue or green) — the standard
- **Concrete** — found at some older or budget facilities
- **Carpet** — some indoor venues

### Quality indicators

When visiting a new court, look for:

- Even, well-maintained surface (no patches, tears, or uneven sand distribution)
- Clean glass (dirty glass affects ball rebound and visibility)
- Proper lighting (no dark spots or glare)
- Intact mesh fencing (no holes or sagging)
- Functional net (correct height, no excessive sag)
- Good drainage on outdoor courts (standing water after rain is a red flag)

## Booking practicalities

### When to book

**Peak times** (highest demand, highest prices):

- Weekday evenings (18:00–22:00)
- Saturday and Sunday mornings (09:00–13:00)

**Off-peak times** (easier to book, often cheaper):

- Weekday mornings and early afternoons
- Sunday evenings
- Summer months in hot climates (everyone plays early morning or late evening)

### How far in advance

- **Popular urban courts:** book 2–7 days ahead for peak times
- **Suburban or less busy clubs:** same-day booking often available
- **League and tournament bookings:** can lock up courts weeks in advance

### Pricing

Booking rates vary enormously by location:

- **Spain:** €10–€30 per 90-minute session (4 players split the cost)
- **UK and Northern Europe:** €30–€70 per session
- **UAE and Middle East:** €30–€80 per session
- **US and Canada:** $40–$100 per session
- **Argentina:** significantly cheaper than European rates

Cost is split among four players. A €60 booking is €15 per person for 90 minutes of exercise and social time — competitive with most fitness activities.

### Cancellation

- **Free cancellation** up to 12–24 hours before the booking
- **Partial refund** for cancellations inside that window
- **No refund** for no-shows
- Check the specific policy before booking, especially for expensive courts

## What to expect at your first session

### What to bring

- **A padel racket.** If you don't own one, most clubs rent rackets for a small fee (typically €3–€10). Some clubs include racket rental in the court price.
- **Padel or tennis shoes.** Court shoes with non-marking soles are essential. Running shoes don't provide adequate lateral support.
- **Comfortable sportswear.** Standard athletic clothing. No specific dress code at most recreational facilities.
- **Water.** Courts can get hot, especially indoor facilities and outdoor courts in warm climates.
- **Balls.** Some clubs include balls with the court booking. Others require you to buy a can at reception. A can of three runs €4–€8.

### What happens on court

If you're a complete beginner:

1. **The serve is underhand.** Bounce the ball and hit it below waist height. Serve diagonally into the opposite service box.
2. **The walls are in play.** After the ball bounces on your side, it can hit the glass walls and you can still play it. The most distinctive feature of padel.
3. **You play doubles.** Padel is always 4 players (2 vs 2). One player covers the left side, the other the right.
4. **Scoring is like tennis.** 15, 30, 40, game. First to 6 games wins the set. Most social matches play one or two sets.
5. **Positioning matters more than power.** Padel rewards placement, patience, and teamwork over brute force. The best position is at the net.

### Etiquette

- **Arrive on time.** Court bookings are fixed slots; showing up late cuts into your playing time.
- **Warm up briefly.** A few minutes of gentle hitting before you start scoring.
- **Communicate with your partner.** Call "mine" or "yours" to avoid confusion.
- **Retrieve balls that enter neighbouring courts** at a natural break in play. Never walk onto an adjacent court during a point.
- **Leave the court clean.** Pick up ball cans, water bottles, and any trash.
- **Keep noise reasonable.** Excessive shouting or racket throwing is frowned upon everywhere.

## What if you don't have four players?

Padel needs four. Common fixes:

### Open play and Americano events

Many clubs run **open play sessions** or **Americano tournaments** where individual players sign up and are randomly paired. The best way to meet other players and integrate into the local padel community.

### Playtomic player matching

Playtomic's match feature lets you create an "open match" where other players can join to fill out a foursome. You set the level, time, and court; the app does the rest.

### Social media groups

Facebook groups, WhatsApp groups, and local community boards often run active padel communities. Search for "[Your City] padel" on Facebook, ask at your local club's reception about WhatsApp groups, check Reddit (r/padel) or local sports forums.

### Club leagues and programmes

Most clubs run weekly leagues, ladders, or group coaching sessions. Structured ways to play regularly, meet other players, and improve your game.

## Country-specific notes

**Spain.** Courts are abundant but very busy at peak times. Book ahead for evening slots. Many municipal sports centres offer courts at subsidised rates.

**United Kingdom.** Court count has grown dramatically but still trails demand. Book well ahead. Indoor courts are standard given the climate.

**United States.** Padel is still emerging. Courts are concentrated in Miami, New York, Los Angeles, Houston, Dallas. Expect premium rates. Some tennis clubs are converting courts.

**UAE and Middle East.** Indoor facilities dominate due to the heat. Excellent quality courts, often as part of larger sports or leisure complexes. Prices are moderate to high.

**Scandinavia.** Indoor courts are standard. Matchi is the primary booking platform across the Nordics. The padel community is large and enthusiastic.
