# Padel in the Olympics: What We Know About Its Olympic Future

Padel is played by over 30 million people across more than 110 countries. It is the fastest-growing sport in the world. It has a professional tour with global broadcast deals, a recognized international federation, and exploding participation numbers on every continent. And yet, padel has never been part of the Olympic Games.

That could change. The sport's governing body, the International Padel Federation (FIP), has mounted a sustained campaign for Olympic inclusion, with the 2032 Brisbane Games as the primary target. Here is everything we know about where padel stands in the Olympic conversation, what hurdles remain, and what the timeline looks like.

## Current Status: Where Padel Stands with the IOC

### ARISF Recognition

The International Padel Federation has achieved recognition through the **Association of IOC Recognised International Sports Federations (ARISF)**. This is a crucial milestone because ARISF membership means the IOC formally acknowledges padel as a sport governed by a legitimate international federation.

However, ARISF recognition is not the same as being on the Olympic program. It is a prerequisite, not a guarantee. Many ARISF-recognized sports (such as squash, cricket, and chess) have sought Olympic inclusion for years or decades without success, though cricket was added for the 2028 Los Angeles Games, showing the path is real.

### What ARISF Recognition Means
- The IOC recognizes the FIP as the legitimate governing body of padel
- Padel is eligible for consideration for future Olympic programs
- The FIP can participate in IOC-organized events and meetings
- It demonstrates that padel meets basic governance, anti-doping, and organizational standards

### What Is Still Needed
ARISF recognition opens the door, but the IOC evaluates several additional criteria before adding a sport to the Olympic program:

- **Global reach:** The sport must be practiced widely across multiple continents and countries
- **Gender equality:** Equal or near-equal participation and competition structures for men and women
- **Youth appeal:** The IOC prioritizes sports that attract younger audiences and participants
- **Broadcasting and commercial viability:** The sport must be televisable and commercially attractive
- **Infrastructure requirements:** Venue and equipment needs must be manageable within the Olympic host city
- **Anti-doping compliance:** Full adherence to WADA standards
- **Governance standards:** Transparent, modern governance structures

## The Brisbane 2032 Target

### Why 2032?

The FIP has focused its Olympic ambitions on the **2032 Brisbane Games** for several strategic reasons:

1. **Timeline alignment:** Under the Olympic Charter (Rule 45), the program for a Games edition is typically finalized approximately seven years before the event. For Brisbane 2032, that means key decisions cluster around 2025-2026.

2. **One-year extension:** The IOC Executive Committee granted a one-year extension to the standard deadline, offering a strategic window in 2025-2026 for new sports to be considered for Brisbane 2032.

3. **Program flexibility:** The IOC has adopted a more flexible approach to the Olympic program, allowing host cities to propose new sports. Brisbane 2032 organizers can recommend sports for inclusion, and padel is reportedly among those being considered.

4. **Growth trajectory:** By 2032, padel's global participation numbers, infrastructure, and professional ecosystem will have had another six years of growth, strengthening the case further.

### The Decision Timeline

The IOC Executive Committee announced that the **initial program for the Brisbane 2032 Games would be decided during an IOC session in 2026**. This makes the current year critical for padel's Olympic aspirations. If padel is included in the initial list of sports under consideration, it moves significantly closer to reality.

However, the final program may not be locked in until closer to the Games. The IOC's new approach allows for some flexibility in adding or adjusting sports.

## The FIP's Campaign

The International Padel Federation has been actively lobbying for Olympic inclusion on multiple fronts:

### Governance Reforms
The FIP has modernized its governance to meet IOC standards, including implementing transparent election processes, financial reporting, and anti-doping programs aligned with WADA requirements.

### Global Expansion
The FIP has aggressively expanded padel's global footprint, supporting the development of national federations in Africa, Asia, and Oceania. This addresses one of the IOC's key criteria: the sport must be genuinely global, not concentrated in a handful of countries.

### Asian Games Inclusion
In a significant milestone, padel was **recognized by the Olympic Council of Asia (OCA)** in November 2025. The sport will be included as an official discipline in the **2026 Asian Games in Aichi-Nagoya, Japan** and the **2026 Asian Beach Games in Sanya, China**.

This is strategically important because:
- It demonstrates that padel can function within a multi-sport event framework
- It shows recognition by a continental Olympic committee
- It provides a precedent that the IOC can point to when evaluating padel's readiness

### Multi-Sport Events
Padel has been featured in several multi-sport events beyond the Asian Games:
- **European Games** consideration
- **World Games** inclusion
- **South American Games** featuring padel as a medal sport
- Various continental championships organized by the FIP's regional bodies

### Broadcasting and Commercial Growth
The Premier Padel tour's broadcast deals, including the recent **beIN Sports agreement covering 39 territories** for the 2026 season and the **Qatar Airways title sponsorship**, demonstrate the commercial viability the IOC values.

## Arguments For Olympic Inclusion

### In Padel's Favor

**Explosive growth.** Over 30 million players in 110+ countries. Projected 70,000 courts globally by 2026. The sport's growth trajectory is among the strongest of any non-Olympic sport.

**Youth appeal.** Padel's demographic skews younger than tennis or golf. The sport's social nature, accessibility, and fast-paced action align perfectly with the IOC's desire to attract younger audiences.

**Gender equality.** Professional padel has parallel men's and women's tours with growing prize money parity. The sport's mixed-doubles format is also popular and could feature at the Olympics.

**Compact venue requirements.** Padel courts are smaller than tennis courts and can be set up in temporary structures. A padel venue for the Olympics would require less space than many existing Olympic sports.

**Broadcasting quality.** Padel's enclosed court, fast rallies, and wall-play drama make for compelling television. The sport is inherently visual and spectator-friendly.

**Institutional backing.** The FIP's partnership with Qatar Sports Investments and the Premier Padel tour's professional infrastructure demonstrate that padel has the organizational capacity to deliver at the Olympic level.

### Potential Challenges

**Concentrated participation.** While padel is growing globally, the sport remains heavily concentrated in Spain, Argentina, and a handful of European countries. The IOC prefers sports with deep participation across all five continents.

**Infrastructure in developing nations.** Padel requires purpose-built courts with glass walls, which are more expensive and complex than, say, a running track or a swimming pool. This could limit the sport's reach in lower-income countries.

**Competition with other sports.** Padel is not the only sport seeking Olympic inclusion. The IOC must balance the size of the Olympic program against viewer and athlete interest, and established sports also lobby to remain on the program.

**Sport federation politics.** The relationship between padel and tennis at the governance level has been complex. The International Tennis Federation (ITF) has occasionally sought to bring padel under its umbrella, which could complicate the FIP's independent bid.

## What Brisbane 2032 Could Look Like

If padel is included in the 2032 Brisbane Olympics, here is what the format might look like:

- **Events:** Men's doubles, women's doubles, and potentially mixed doubles
- **Venue:** A purpose-built or temporary padel facility with multiple courts, likely including a center court with seating for several thousand spectators
- **Format:** A 16 or 32-pair draw with group stages and knockout rounds, played over approximately 10 days
- **Athlete quota:** Likely capped at 48-64 athletes (24-32 pairs) per gender event
- **Qualification:** Through FIP world rankings and continental representation quotas

## Other Olympic Pathway: Los Angeles 2028

While Brisbane 2032 remains the primary target, there was brief discussion about padel as a host-city proposed sport for the **2028 Los Angeles Games**. However, LA 2028 organizers chose cricket, flag football, lacrosse, squash, and baseball/softball as their additional sports. Padel was not included.

This is not necessarily a setback. The LA 2028 decision was made before padel's most recent growth surge, and the 2032 timeline gives the sport more time to strengthen its case.

## The Bigger Picture: What Olympic Status Would Mean

Olympic inclusion would be transformative for padel:

- **Legitimacy:** The Olympics confer a level of global recognition that no other sporting event can match
- **Funding:** National Olympic committees would allocate funding for padel programs, accelerating development in countries where the sport is still emerging
- **Media exposure:** Olympic broadcasts reach billions of viewers, introducing padel to audiences who have never seen the sport
- **Grassroots growth:** Olympic status drives participation at the recreational level, as countries develop programs to identify and train potential Olympic athletes
- **Sponsorship:** Olympic sports attract a broader range of corporate sponsors

## Wrapping Up

Padel's Olympic journey is at a critical juncture. The sport has achieved ARISF recognition, secured a place in the Asian Games, built a professional tour with global reach, and continues to grow at a rate that few sports can match. Whether padel makes it to Brisbane 2032 or has to wait for a later Games, the trajectory is clear. The question is not if padel becomes an Olympic sport, but when. And every court built, every match broadcast, and every new player who picks up a pala brings that moment closer.
