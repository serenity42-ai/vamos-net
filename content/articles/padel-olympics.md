# Padel in the Olympics: What We Know About Its Olympic Future

Padel has 35 million players across 150 countries. It has a professional tour with global broadcast deals, an internationally recognised federation, and explosive participation growth on every continent.

It has never been in the Olympics.

That could change. The International Padel Federation (FIP) has been running a sustained campaign for Olympic inclusion, with the **2032 Brisbane Games** as the primary target. Here's where things actually stand.

## Current status with the IOC

### ARISF recognition

Padel's governing body has recognition through the **Association of IOC Recognised International Sports Federations (ARISF)**. ARISF membership means the IOC formally acknowledges padel as a sport governed by a legitimate international federation.

ARISF recognition is not the same as being on the Olympic programme. It is a prerequisite, not a guarantee. Many ARISF-recognised sports (squash, cricket, chess) sought Olympic inclusion for years or decades without success. Cricket made it for Los Angeles 2028, which proves the path is real; it doesn't make it short.

### What ARISF recognition means

- The IOC recognises the FIP as the legitimate governing body of padel
- Padel is eligible for consideration for future Olympic programmes
- The FIP can participate in IOC-organised events and meetings
- It demonstrates that padel meets baseline governance, anti-doping, and organisational standards

### What is still needed

ARISF opens the door. The IOC then evaluates several criteria before adding a sport to the Olympic programme:

- **Global reach.** The sport must be practised widely across multiple continents and countries.
- **Gender equality.** Equal or near-equal participation and competition structures for men and women.
- **Youth appeal.** The IOC prioritises sports that attract younger audiences and participants.
- **Broadcasting and commercial viability.** The sport must be televisable and commercially viable.
- **Infrastructure.** Venue and equipment needs must fit within the host city's plan.
- **Anti-doping compliance.** Full adherence to WADA standards.
- **Governance.** Transparent, modern federation governance.

## The Brisbane 2032 target

### Why 2032

Several reasons the FIP has focused there:

1. **Timeline alignment.** Under Olympic Charter Rule 45, the programme for a Games edition is typically finalised about seven years before the event. For Brisbane 2032, key decisions cluster around 2025–2026.

2. **One-year extension.** The IOC Executive Committee granted a one-year extension to the standard deadline, opening a strategic window in 2025–2026 for new sports to be considered.

3. **Programme flexibility.** The IOC has shifted to a more flexible approach, letting host cities propose new sports. Brisbane 2032 organisers can recommend sports for inclusion, and padel is reportedly among those being considered.

4. **Growth trajectory.** By 2032, padel will have had another six years of participation, infrastructure, and professional growth, strengthening the case further.

### The decision timeline

The IOC announced that the **initial programme for Brisbane 2032 would be decided at an IOC session in 2026**. Which makes this year critical for padel. If padel is included in the initial sports list, it moves significantly closer to reality.

The final programme may not be locked in until closer to the Games. The IOC's new flexibility allows for some adjustment in the years after the initial decision.

## The FIP's campaign

Lobbying has been active on multiple fronts.

### Governance reforms

Governance reforms have brought padel up to IOC standards: transparent election processes, financial reporting, and anti-doping programmes aligned with WADA requirements.

### Global expansion

Padel's footprint has expanded aggressively, with the FIP supporting national federations in Africa, Asia, and Oceania. This addresses one of the IOC's key criteria: the sport must be genuinely global, not concentrated in a handful of countries.

### Asian Games inclusion

A milestone moment. Padel was **recognised by the Olympic Council of Asia (OCA) in November 2025** and will be included in the **2026 Asian Games in Aichi-Nagoya, Japan**, plus the **2026 Asian Beach Games in Sanya, China**.

This matters strategically:

- It demonstrates padel can function within a multi-sport event framework
- It shows recognition by a continental Olympic committee
- It gives the IOC a precedent to reference when evaluating padel's readiness

### Other multi-sport events

Padel has been featured in several events beyond the Asian Games:

- European Games consideration
- World Games inclusion
- South American Games featuring padel as a medal sport
- Various continental championships organised by the FIP's regional bodies

### Broadcasting and commercial growth

The Premier Padel tour's broadcast deals, including the **beIN Sports agreement covering 39 territories** for the 2026 season and the **Qatar Airways title sponsorship**, demonstrate the commercial viability the IOC values.

## Arguments for inclusion

### What's working in padel's favour

**Explosive growth.** 35 million players in 150+ countries. 70,000 courts globally projected by 2026. The trajectory is among the strongest of any non-Olympic sport.

**Youth appeal.** Padel's demographic skews younger than tennis or golf. Social, accessible, fast-paced, exactly the profile the IOC says it wants in new sports.

**Gender equality.** Parallel men's and women's tours with rising prize-money parity. The mixed-doubles format is popular and could feature at the Olympics.

**Compact venue requirements.** Padel courts are smaller than tennis courts and can be set up in temporary structures. A padel venue requires less space than many existing Olympic sports.

**Broadcast quality.** The enclosed court, fast rallies, and wall-play drama make for good television. The sport is inherently visual and spectator-friendly.

**Institutional backing.** The FIP's partnership with Qatar Sports Investments and Premier Padel's professional infrastructure demonstrate the organisational capacity to deliver at Olympic level.

### What's working against

**Concentrated participation.** Padel is growing globally, but the sport remains heavily concentrated in Spain, Argentina, and a handful of European countries. The IOC prefers sports with deep participation across all five continents.

**Infrastructure in developing nations.** Padel requires purpose-built courts with glass walls, more expensive and complex than a running track or a swimming pool. That could limit the sport's reach in lower-income countries on a meaningful timeline.

**Competition with other sports.** Padel is not the only sport seeking Olympic inclusion. The IOC must balance programme size against viewer and athlete interest, and established sports also lobby to remain on the programme.

**Federation politics.** The relationship between padel and tennis at governance level has been complex. The International Tennis Federation has occasionally sought to bring padel under its umbrella, which could complicate the FIP's independent bid.

## What Brisbane 2032 could look like

If padel makes it to Brisbane, here's the likely format:

- **Events:** men's doubles, women's doubles, possibly mixed doubles
- **Venue:** purpose-built or temporary padel facility with multiple courts, including a centre court with seating for several thousand spectators
- **Format:** a 16- or 32-pair draw, group stages plus knockout rounds, played over approximately 10 days
- **Athlete quota:** likely capped at 48–64 athletes (24–32 pairs) per gender event
- **Qualification:** through FIP world rankings and continental representation quotas

## What about LA 2028?

There was brief discussion of padel as a host-city proposed sport for **Los Angeles 2028**. The LA 2028 organisers chose cricket, flag football, lacrosse, squash, and baseball/softball as their additional sports. Padel was not included.

Not necessarily a setback. The LA 2028 decision was made before padel's most recent growth surge, and the 2032 timeline gives the sport more time to strengthen its case.

## What Olympic status would mean

Olympic inclusion would change the sport meaningfully:

- **Legitimacy.** The Olympics confer a level of global recognition no other sporting event matches.
- **Funding.** National Olympic committees would allocate funding for padel programmes, accelerating development in countries where the sport is still emerging.
- **Media exposure.** Olympic broadcasts reach billions of viewers, introducing padel to audiences who have never seen the sport.
- **Grassroots growth.** Olympic status drives recreational participation, as countries build programmes to identify and train potential Olympic athletes.
- **Sponsorship.** Olympic sports attract a broader range of corporate sponsors than even the most successful private circuit.
