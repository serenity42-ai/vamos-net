# Padel Scoring System: A Complete Breakdown

If you have ever watched or played tennis, you already understand the vast majority of padel scoring. The two sports share nearly identical scoring structures, from the familiar 15-30-40 game progression to the set and match format. But padel introduces a few wrinkles of its own, most notably the golden point rule, that can catch newcomers off guard.

This guide covers everything you need to know about how scoring works in padel, whether you are stepping onto the court for the first time or trying to follow a Premier Padel broadcast.

## The Basics: Points, Games, and Sets

Padel uses the same point-by-point scoring system as tennis. Each game progresses through the following sequence:

- **0 (Love)** - No points scored
- **15** - First point won
- **30** - Second point won
- **40** - Third point won
- **Game** - Fourth point won (if leading)

A pair needs to win four points to take a game, provided they lead by at least one point after both sides reach 40. When the score reaches 40-40, the game is at **deuce**, and the rules for resolving that tie depend on whether the match uses the advantage system or the golden point rule (more on both below).

### How Sets Work

A set is won by the first pair to reach **six games**, with a minimum lead of two games. So a set can end 6-0, 6-1, 6-2, 6-3, or 6-4 without any complications.

If the set reaches 5-5, the pair that wins the next game to make it 6-5 puts pressure on the opposing pair. If the trailing pair responds to level at 6-6, a **tiebreak** is played to decide the set.

### How Matches Work

A standard padel match is played as the **best of three sets**. The first pair to win two sets takes the match. There is no best-of-five format in padel, even at the professional level. This keeps matches relatively compact, typically lasting between 60 and 120 minutes, which is one reason the sport translates so well to both live viewing and broadcast schedules.

## Deuce and the Advantage System

In traditional padel scoring, when a game reaches 40-40 (deuce), the pair that wins the next point gains the **advantage**. If that same pair wins the following point, they take the game. If the opposing pair wins the point instead, the score returns to deuce.

Under the advantage system, a game at deuce can theoretically continue indefinitely, with the score swinging back and forth until one pair manages to win two consecutive points from deuce.

The receiving pair chooses which side of the court (left or right) to receive the serve when the score reaches deuce. This is a strategic decision, as the left side (the backhand side for right-handed players) is traditionally where the stronger returner positions themselves.

## The Golden Point Rule

The golden point rule is one of the most distinctive features of professional padel. Introduced to speed up matches and add tension, the golden point replaces the traditional advantage system at deuce.

Here is how it works:

1. When the score reaches 40-40 (deuce), only **one deciding point** is played
2. The **receiving pair** chooses which side of the court to receive the serve
3. Whoever wins that single point wins the game

There is no advantage, no need to win two consecutive points. One point decides everything. This creates enormous pressure on both the serving and receiving pairs, and it often produces the most dramatic moments in professional padel.

### Where the Golden Point Is Used

The golden point rule is used in the **Premier Padel** circuit and was previously standard on the World Padel Tour (WPT). Most professional tournaments now follow this format.

However, not every padel match uses the golden point. Many amateur leagues, club competitions, and recreational matches still use the traditional advantage system. If you are playing socially, it is worth clarifying with your opponents before the match which system you are using.

### Strategic Implications

The golden point fundamentally changes the dynamics of a game at deuce:

- **For the serving pair:** There is added pressure because a single lapse can cost the game, regardless of how well they have served throughout
- **For the receiving pair:** The ability to choose which side to receive on is a significant tactical advantage. Pairs typically place their stronger returner on the chosen side
- **Match tempo:** The golden point prevents games from dragging on through multiple deuces, keeping the overall match length more predictable

## The Tiebreak

When a set reaches 6-6, a tiebreak is played to determine the winner. The padel tiebreak follows standard tennis tiebreak rules:

1. The pair that was due to serve starts with **one serve** from the right (deuce) side
2. After that, each pair serves **two consecutive points**, alternating between the right and left sides
3. Players switch ends every **six points**
4. The first pair to reach **seven points** with a lead of at least two wins the tiebreak and the set
5. If the tiebreak reaches 6-6, play continues until one pair leads by two points

The tiebreak is scored numerically (1, 2, 3, 4, etc.) rather than using the 15-30-40 system. A tiebreak score might read 7-5 or 9-7, for example.

In some tournament formats, if the match reaches one set all and the deciding set also goes to 6-6, a **super tiebreak** (first to 10 points, win by two) may be played instead of a standard tiebreak. However, this is not universal and depends on the specific competition rules.

## Service Rules and Scoring

Understanding the serve is essential to understanding the scoring flow in padel:

- The serve must be hit **underhand**, with the ball struck at or below waist height
- The server stands behind the service line and serves diagonally into the opponent's service box
- The ball must bounce once in the service box before the returner hits it
- If the ball hits the net and lands in the correct service box, it is a **let** and the serve is replayed
- If the ball hits the net and then touches the side or back wall before bouncing in the service box, it is a **fault**
- Each server gets **two attempts** (first serve and second serve)
- A **double fault** (two consecutive faults) results in the loss of the point

The serving pair alternates servers each game, and within a game, the server remains the same. The order of serving within a pair is established at the beginning of each set and must be maintained throughout that set.

## Match Formats in Professional Padel

Professional padel tournaments follow a consistent format:

### Premier Padel Tour

- Best of three sets
- Golden point at deuce
- Standard tiebreak at 6-6 in each set
- No final set super tiebreak (standard tiebreak applies)

### Club and Amateur Matches

Recreational and club-level padel often uses variations:

- **Best of three sets** with advantage scoring (no golden point)
- **Single set to 9 games** with a tiebreak at 8-8 (common for time-limited bookings)
- **Timed matches** where the pair leading when the clock expires wins
- **Round-robin formats** using shortened sets (first to 4 or 6 games)

These variations exist primarily to fit matches into standard court booking windows, which are typically 90 minutes.

## Scoring Summary at a Glance

For quick reference, here is the complete scoring structure:

**Points in a game:** 0 (Love), 15, 30, 40, Game

**Games in a set:** First to 6 games, win by 2. Tiebreak at 6-6.

**Sets in a match:** Best of 3 sets. First to win 2 sets takes the match.

**At deuce (40-40):**
- Golden point: one deciding point, receiving pair chooses side
- Advantage system: must win two consecutive points from deuce

**Tiebreak:** First to 7 points, win by 2. Numerical scoring.

## Common Scoring Scenarios Explained

### Scenario 1: A Straightforward Game
The serving pair wins four points in a row. The score goes 15-0, 30-0, 40-0, Game. This is called a "love game" or "game to love."

### Scenario 2: Deuce with Golden Point
Both pairs trade points, and the score reaches 40-40. Under golden point rules, the receiving pair selects their preferred side. One point is played. The winner takes the game.

### Scenario 3: Tiebreak Set
The set reaches 6-6. A tiebreak is played. Pair A wins 7-4 in the tiebreak. Pair A wins the set 7-6.

### Scenario 4: Deciding Set
The match is tied at one set each. The third set is played with the same rules as the first two. If it reaches 6-6, a standard tiebreak (or super tiebreak, depending on tournament rules) determines the winner.

## How Padel Scoring Differs from Tennis

While the systems are nearly identical, there are a few key differences worth noting:

| Feature | Padel | Tennis |
|---------|-------|--------|
| Golden point | Standard in professional play | Not used (advantage system standard) |
| Match length | Best of 3 sets | Best of 3 or 5 sets |
| Serve style | Underhand only | Overhand |
| Deuce receiving choice | Receiving pair picks side | No side choice |
| Let on wall contact | Fault if ball touches wall after net | N/A (no walls) |

## Tips for Keeping Score

If you are new to padel and keeping score during a match, here are some practical tips:

- **Call the server's score first.** If the serving pair has 30 and the receiving pair has 15, the score is called "30-15."
- **Track games clearly.** Use a simple tally or the scoreboard on the court.
- **Agree on deuce rules before playing.** Decide whether you are using golden point or advantage before the match starts.
- **Remember service order.** Each player serves a full game in turn. The order within a pair stays fixed for the entire set but can change between sets.

## Wrapping Up

Padel scoring is intuitive for anyone with a tennis background and straightforward enough for complete beginners to pick up within a few games. The golden point rule adds a layer of excitement that sets professional padel apart, creating decisive moments that make every deuce a high-stakes affair.

Whether you are watching the Premier Padel tour or heading to the court with friends, understanding the scoring system lets you focus on what matters most: the game itself.
