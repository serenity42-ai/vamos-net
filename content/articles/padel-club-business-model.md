# How Padel Clubs Make Money: Revenue, Costs, and ROI Breakdown

Opening a padel club is one of the most talked-about sports-and-leisure business opportunities of the decade. The pitch is seductive: a fast-growing sport, high court utilisation, a social format that drives repeat visits, compact real estate requirements. The reality on a P&L is more complicated. Capital allocation matters. Operations matter. Local market dynamics matter even more.

Here is what the actual economics look like in 2026: construction costs, revenue streams, operating expenses, breakeven timelines, and the franchise models gaining traction across the US and Europe.

## The cost to build

Construction cost is where every padel club business plan starts, and the range is wider than newcomers expect.

### Court construction

A single padel court (the structure itself: glass walls, metal frame, artificial turf, lighting, basic drainage) typically costs **$25,000–$70,000** depending on quality, manufacturer, and region (MyPadelWay, January 2026; Rex Operator Guide, 2025). A more granular breakdown:

**Outdoor courts:**

- Basic / economy court (emerging markets, Chinese manufacturers): $18,000–$30,000
- Mid-range panoramic court (European manufacturers): $35,000–$55,000
- Premium panoramic court (top-tier brands like Mondo, MejorSet): $50,000–$70,000

**Indoor courts:**

Add the building or canopy structure, HVAC, insulation, and higher-grade lighting. A fully enclosed indoor court can cost $80,000–$150,000 once you factor in the building itself, depending on whether you're retrofitting an existing warehouse or building from scratch (BusinessDojo, June 2025; Luckin Padel, 2025).

### Beyond the court structure

The court itself is only part of the capital expenditure. A full club build-out typically includes:

- **Site preparation and civil works.** Foundation, grading, drainage. $10,000–$40,000 per court depending on terrain.
- **Lighting systems.** LED floodlighting runs $3,000–$8,000 per court.
- **Fencing and landscaping.** $5,000–$15,000 for outdoor facilities.
- **Clubhouse and reception.** Reception area, changing rooms, bathrooms, lounge. From $30,000 for a basic setup to $200,000+ for a premium facility.
- **Pro shop fit-out.** $10,000–$30,000.
- **F&B infrastructure.** Kitchen, bar, terrace seating. $50,000–$200,000 depending on ambition.
- **Technology.** Booking system integration, access control, camera/streaming setup. $5,000–$20,000.
- **Permits and professional fees.** Architectural plans, engineering, local permits. $10,000–$50,000.

### Total capital requirements

For a typical 4–6 court outdoor club with a modest clubhouse, budget **$250,000–$500,000** in total capital expenditure. A 6–8 court indoor facility with full amenities can require **$500,000–$1.5 million** or more. Premium destination clubs like the 18-court Los Naranjos Padel Club in Marbella, which includes a gym, restaurant, pro shop, and physiotherapy clinic, represent multi-million-dollar investments (Padel Business Magazine, December 2025).

According to Sheets.Market's 2025 padel club business model analysis, operators achieving 30–35% EBITDA margins typically work within the $250,000–$500,000 CapEx range.

## Revenue streams

Successful padel clubs are not single-revenue businesses. The most profitable operators layer multiple income streams on top of court rental, which remains the foundation but rarely accounts for more than 50–60% of total revenue in well-run facilities.

### 1. Court rentals (50–65% of revenue)

The core revenue driver. Hourly court rental rates vary significantly by market:

- **Spain:** €4–€7/hour for public courts; €20–€40/hour for private clubs (peak hours)
- **Italy:** €25–€50/hour
- **France:** €30–€50/hour
- **Netherlands:** €25–€40/hour
- **Sweden:** €30–€55/hour
- **United Kingdom:** £40–£80/hour (London clubs at the premium end; Padium in London charges £80/hour)
- **United States:** $40–$80/hour
- **Middle East (UAE, Qatar):** $40–$70/hour

Courts typically operate 12–16 bookable hours per day. In mature markets, well-located clubs achieve 50–70% average utilisation across the full week, with peak hours (evenings and weekends) often at 90–100% and off-peak significantly lower.

A realistic revenue estimate per court:

- Conservative (40% utilisation, €30/hour average): ~€52,000/year
- Moderate (55% utilisation, €35/hour average): ~€84,000/year
- Optimistic (70% utilisation, €40/hour average): ~€122,000/year

Unix Padel's investment report models a 3-court facility at just 5 hours daily bookings at €30/hour generating €162,000 annually, or €54,000 per court (Unix Padel, 2025). That aligns with the conservative end of industry estimates.

### 2. Coaching and academies (10–15% of revenue)

Group clinics, private lessons, and structured academy programmes are high-margin revenue. Coaching rates typically range from €30–€60 per player for group sessions (4–8 players) to €50–€100+ for private coaching. A club with 2–3 resident coaches can generate €50,000–€150,000 annually in coaching revenue, with strong margins once coaches are salaried rather than taking hourly splits.

### 3. Memberships (5–15% of revenue)

Membership models vary widely:

- **Annual club memberships:** €100–€500/year, typically providing booking priority, discounted rates, or included off-peak hours
- **Monthly subscriptions:** €30–€80/month for unlimited or frequent play packages
- **Premium tiers:** €500–€1,000+/year including locker rental, guest passes, and event access

Memberships provide recurring revenue and improve cash-flow predictability. In the Netherlands, some clubs offer memberships as low as €160/year with unlimited play, relying on volume and ancillary spend (Reddit community reports, 2024). In premium markets like London or Dubai, memberships can exceed £1,000/year.

### 4. Food and beverage (10–20% of revenue)

The social nature of padel makes F&B a natural revenue stream. Players typically spend 30–60 minutes at the club before or after matches. Clubs with quality bar and restaurant offerings can generate €5–€15 per visitor in F&B spend. For a club handling 100–200 daily players, that translates to €200,000–€600,000+ annually in F&B revenue.

As padel business consultant Manuel Romaguera noted in Padel Business Magazine: "A sustainable padel business begins by understanding the lifestyle, expectations and behaviour of the people you want to attract. Courts are the centrepiece, but the value is generated in everything that surrounds them" (Padel Business Magazine, December 2025).

### 5. Pro shop and equipment retail (3–8% of revenue)

Rackets, balls, shoes, grips, bags, apparel. Margins on padel equipment typically run 30–50%. A well-stocked pro shop in a busy club can generate €30,000–€100,000 in annual sales. Racket demo programmes and partnerships with brands like Bullpadel, Head, Wilson, or Nox can add wholesale margin.

### 6. Events, tournaments, and corporate hire (5–10% of revenue)

Social tournaments, corporate team-building events, birthday parties, league play. Corporate bookings command premium rates (typically 50–100% above standard court rental) and often include F&B packages. A club running 2–4 events per month can generate €20,000–€80,000 in additional annual revenue.

### 7. Ancillary revenue

Smaller but meaningful streams:

- **Locker rental:** €5–€15/month per locker
- **Equipment rental:** racket hire at €3–€5 per session
- **Sponsorship and advertising:** court-side advertising, branded events
- **Video/streaming services:** match recording and analysis (some clubs charge €5–€10 per match for automated video)

## Operating costs

Realistic profitability projections live or die on the cost structure.

### Major cost categories (% of revenue)

- **Rent or mortgage:** 15–30% (the single largest variable; location-dependent)
- **Staff:** 20–30% (reception, maintenance, coaching staff, management)
- **Utilities:** 5–10% (lighting, HVAC for indoor facilities, water)
- **Court maintenance:** 3–5% (turf replacement every 5–7 years at €3,000–€6,000 per court, glass cleaning, structural upkeep)
- **Technology and software:** 2–4% (booking platform fees, POS systems, website)
- **Marketing:** 3–7% (digital marketing, local advertising, events)
- **Insurance:** 1–3%
- **Consumables and supplies:** 2–3%

### Playtomic and booking platform fees

Most clubs use booking platforms, and Playtomic dominates the market with its matchmaking and court management features. Platform fees are a real cost. Some operators report paying over $5,000 annually in Playtomic fees, particularly for skill-based match bookings facilitated through the app (Reddit, July 2025). Clubs must weigh customer acquisition benefits against the commission cost, typically 10–20% of bookings made through the platform.

Playtomic reported €346 million transacted through its platform as of September 2025, growing 51% year-over-year (Crowdcube, November 2025). The platform serves as both a booking tool and a player acquisition channel, making it nearly essential for new clubs but a cost mature clubs with strong direct bookings may seek to minimise.

### Typical P&L for a 6-court club

A well-run 6-court club in a mid-tier European market might look like this:

**Annual revenue:** €400,000–€600,000

- Court rentals: €250,000–€350,000
- Coaching: €40,000–€80,000
- Memberships: €30,000–€60,000
- F&B: €50,000–€80,000
- Pro shop and other: €30,000–€50,000

**Annual operating costs:** €280,000–€400,000

- Rent: €80,000–€150,000
- Staff: €80,000–€120,000
- Utilities: €25,000–€50,000
- Maintenance: €15,000–€25,000
- Marketing and technology: €20,000–€35,000
- Other: €15,000–€25,000

**EBITDA:** €100,000–€200,000 (25–35% margin)

## ROI and breakeven timeline

The breakeven question is the one every prospective operator asks first. The answer depends heavily on initial investment, rent structure, and local demand.

**Operational breakeven** (the point at which monthly revenue covers monthly operating costs) is typically achievable within 12–18 months for a well-located club with effective pre-launch marketing and community building. Rex's Operator Guide suggests most clubs aim for 18–30 months (Rex, 2025).

**Full capital recovery** takes longer. Financial Models Lab projects operational breakeven around 14 months but notes that full return on initial capital investment can take 56 months (roughly 4.5 years) for a full-service padel centre (Financial Models Lab, December 2025).

Key variables affecting ROI:

- **Location quality.** Proximity to residential density, commuter access, parking.
- **Court utilisation.** Every 10% improvement in utilisation dramatically impacts profitability.
- **Rent structure.** Clubs that own their real estate have fundamentally different economics than those leasing.
- **Seasonality.** Indoor facilities in northern climates have more consistent year-round revenue.
- **Ancillary revenue development.** Clubs that invest in F&B, coaching programmes, and events break even faster.

## Franchise models

The franchise model is gaining traction as a way to scale padel operations while reducing risk for individual operators.

### Conquer Padel (US)

The most prominent padel franchise in the United States. Conquer Padel opened its flagship club in Tempe, Arizona in 2025 and announced plans for 50 locations within a year. Signed franchisees in New Jersey and Orlando. Raised $15 million in a Reg D 506(c) private equity offering with a minimum investment of $50,000 (BusinessWire, September 2025; March 2025). Positions itself as a turnkey franchise model combining real estate, sport, and lifestyle.

### P1 Padel (US)

Described as the first registered padel franchise in the United States. P1 focuses on replicating a club model that combines sport with social experience (Interlink FBC, October 2025).

### Padelcreations (Europe)

A European court manufacturer and consultancy that offers end-to-end club development services, including business planning, court construction, and operational support. Their model is less a traditional franchise and more a turnkey development partnership (Padelcreations, 2025).

### European chain operators

In Spain, operators like Padel Nuestro have built networks combining retail, coaching, and club operations. In the UK, Game4Padel and We Are Padel have scaled multi-location operations. In Sweden, Padel Zenter and others have built substantial court networks leveraging the country's high per-capita padel adoption.

## Success patterns and failure modes

### What works

**Community-first operators.** Clubs that invest in community building (leagues, social events, coaching ladders, beginner programmes) achieve higher retention and utilisation. Padel's social DNA rewards operators who understand they are running a community hub, not just renting courts.

**Multi-revenue operators.** Clubs that layer F&B, coaching, retail, and events on top of court rental consistently outperform single-stream operators. The Los Naranjos model in Marbella, treating the club as a hospitality experience comparable to a spa or golf club, represents the premium end of this approach.

**Technology adopters.** Clubs that use platforms like Playtomic for matchmaking and booking, automated court lighting, and data-driven pricing (dynamic peak/off-peak rates) operate more efficiently and attract more players.

**Location discipline.** The three most important factors in padel club success remain location, location, and location. Proximity to dense residential areas with high disposable income is the single strongest predictor of utilisation.

### What fails

**Build-it-and-they-will-come.** Operators who invest heavily in court construction without corresponding investment in community development, marketing, and programming frequently face disappointing utilisation rates.

**Underestimating working capital.** Many club failures stem not from poor demand but from insufficient capital to weather the 12–18 month ramp-up before reaching operational breakeven.

**Outdoor-only in seasonal climates.** Clubs in northern Europe or northern US states that build outdoor-only facilities face 3–5 months of minimal revenue annually. The maths rarely works without an indoor component.

**Oversaturated locations.** In parts of Spain and Italy, the pace of court construction has outrun local demand growth. Opening the fifth padel club within a 5 km radius requires a differentiated value proposition.

**Neglecting F&B.** Courts alone do not create a sticky customer experience. Clubs without comfortable lounge areas and food-and-beverage service miss a significant revenue opportunity and reduce the "hang time" that builds community.

## Club software and booking platforms

The technology layer supporting padel club operations has matured rapidly:

- **Playtomic.** The dominant platform globally, serving thousands of clubs across 30+ countries. Offers court booking, matchmaking, club management, and payment processing. Rafael Nadal serves as brand ambassador. The platform raised $70 million in March 2025 (Sports Business Journal).
- **Rex.** A booking and member management platform positioning itself as an operator-friendly alternative with lower commission structures.
- **MATCHi.** A Swedish-origin platform strong in Scandinavian markets, offering booking, matchmaking, and league management.
- **Kourts.** A newer entrant focused on the US market with padel-specific features.

The choice of booking platform affects both customer acquisition and operating costs. Playtomic's massive user base (estimated at several million active users) provides a powerful customer acquisition channel, but clubs should carefully model the fee impact on their unit economics.

## The verdict for prospective operators

Padel club economics are favourable compared to most sports facility businesses, but they are not the guaranteed goldmine that promotional materials sometimes suggest. The fundamental equation works: strong demand growth, high repeat visit rates, compact real estate requirements, and multiple revenue opportunities create a genuinely attractive business model.

The operators who will thrive in 2026 and beyond are those who approach padel as a hospitality and community business that happens to have courts, rather than a court rental business that happens to serve coffee. The sport's social DNA is its commercial asset. Operators who amplify the social experience will capture disproportionate value as the market continues to grow.

For anyone serious about entering the padel business, the advice is consistent across every successful operator interviewed: invest in community, plan for 18–24 months of ramp-up, diversify your revenue from day one, and never underestimate the importance of a good kitchen.
