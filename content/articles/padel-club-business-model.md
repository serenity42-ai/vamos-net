# How Padel Clubs Make Money: Revenue, Costs, and ROI Breakdown

Opening a padel club is one of the most talked-about business opportunities in the sports and leisure industry. The logic is seductive: a fast-growing sport, high court utilization rates, a social format that drives repeat visits, and relatively compact real estate requirements. But between the enthusiasm and the spreadsheet lies a complex business that demands careful capital allocation, operational discipline, and a clear-eyed understanding of local market dynamics.

This article breaks down the real economics of operating a padel club in 2026 -- from construction costs and revenue streams to breakeven timelines and franchise models.

## The Cost to Build

The starting point for any padel club business plan is construction cost, and the range is wider than most newcomers expect.

### Court Construction Costs

A single padel court -- the structure itself, including glass walls, metal frame, artificial turf, lighting, and basic drainage -- typically costs between $25,000 and $70,000 depending on quality, manufacturer, and region (MyPadelWay, January 2026; Rex Operator Guide, 2025). Here is a more granular breakdown:

**Outdoor courts:**
- Basic/economy court (emerging markets, Chinese manufacturers): $18,000-$30,000
- Mid-range panoramic court (European manufacturers): $35,000-$55,000
- Premium panoramic court (top-tier brands like Mondo, MejorSet): $50,000-$70,000

**Indoor courts:**
Additional costs for indoor facilities include the building or canopy structure, HVAC, insulation, and higher-grade lighting. A fully enclosed indoor court can cost $80,000-$150,000 when factoring in the building itself, depending on whether you are retrofitting an existing warehouse or building from scratch (BusinessDojo, June 2025; Luckin Padel, 2025).

### Beyond the Court Structure

The court itself is only part of the capital expenditure. A full club build-out typically includes:

- **Site preparation and civil works**: Foundation, grading, drainage -- $10,000-$40,000 per court depending on terrain
- **Lighting systems**: LED floodlighting runs $3,000-$8,000 per court
- **Fencing and landscaping**: $5,000-$15,000 for outdoor facilities
- **Clubhouse/reception**: Reception area, changing rooms, bathrooms, lounge -- highly variable, from $30,000 for a basic setup to $200,000+ for a premium facility
- **Pro shop fit-out**: $10,000-$30,000
- **F&B infrastructure**: Kitchen, bar, terrace seating -- $50,000-$200,000 depending on ambition
- **Technology**: Booking system integration, access control, camera/streaming setup -- $5,000-$20,000
- **Permits and professional fees**: Architectural plans, engineering, local permits -- $10,000-$50,000

### Total Capital Requirements

For a typical 4-6 court outdoor club with a modest clubhouse, operators should budget $250,000-$500,000 in total capital expenditure. A 6-8 court indoor facility with full amenities can require $500,000-$1.5 million or more. Premium destination clubs -- like the 18-court Los Naranjos Padel Club in Marbella, which includes a gym, restaurant, pro shop, and physiotherapy clinic -- represent multi-million dollar investments (Padel Business Magazine, December 2025).

According to Sheets.Market's 2025 padel club business model analysis, operators achieving 30-35% EBITDA margins typically work within a $250,000-$500,000 CapEx range.

## Revenue Streams

Successful padel clubs are not single-revenue businesses. The most profitable operators layer multiple income streams on top of court rental, which remains the foundation but rarely accounts for more than 50-60% of total revenue in well-run facilities.

### 1. Court Rentals (50-65% of revenue)

The core revenue driver. Hourly court rental rates vary significantly by market:

- **Spain**: EUR 4-7/hour for public courts; EUR 20-40/hour for private clubs (peak hours)
- **Italy**: EUR 25-50/hour
- **France**: EUR 30-50/hour
- **Netherlands**: EUR 25-40/hour
- **Sweden**: EUR 30-55/hour
- **United Kingdom**: GBP 40-80/hour (London clubs at the premium end; Padium in London charges GBP 80/hour)
- **United States**: $40-80/hour
- **Middle East (UAE, Qatar)**: $40-70/hour

Courts typically operate 12-16 bookable hours per day. In mature markets, well-located clubs achieve 50-70% average utilization across the full week, with peak hours (evenings and weekends) often at 90-100% and off-peak significantly lower.

A realistic revenue estimate per court:
- Conservative (40% utilization, EUR 30/hour average): ~EUR 52,000/year
- Moderate (55% utilization, EUR 35/hour average): ~EUR 84,000/year
- Optimistic (70% utilization, EUR 40/hour average): ~EUR 122,000/year

Unix Padel's investment report models a 3-court facility at just 5 hours daily bookings at EUR 30/hour generating EUR 162,000 annually -- or EUR 54,000 per court (Unix Padel, 2025). This aligns with the conservative end of industry estimates.

### 2. Coaching and Academies (10-15% of revenue)

Group clinics, private lessons, and structured academy programs represent high-margin revenue. Coaching rates typically range from EUR 30-60 per player for group sessions (4-8 players) to EUR 50-100+ for private coaching. A club with 2-3 resident coaches can generate EUR 50,000-150,000 annually in coaching revenue, with strong margins once coaches are salaried rather than taking hourly splits.

### 3. Memberships (5-15% of revenue)

Membership models vary widely:
- **Annual club memberships**: EUR 100-500/year, typically providing booking priority, discounted rates, or included off-peak hours
- **Monthly subscriptions**: EUR 30-80/month for unlimited or frequent play packages
- **Premium tiers**: EUR 500-1,000+/year including locker rental, guest passes, and event access

Memberships provide valuable recurring revenue and improve cash flow predictability. In the Netherlands, some clubs offer memberships as low as EUR 160/year with unlimited play, relying on volume and ancillary spend (Reddit community reports, 2024). In premium markets like London or Dubai, memberships can exceed GBP 1,000/year.

### 4. Food and Beverage (10-20% of revenue)

The social nature of padel makes F&B a natural revenue stream. Players typically spend 30-60 minutes at the club before or after matches. Clubs with quality bar and restaurant offerings can generate EUR 5-15 per visitor in F&B spend. For a club handling 100-200 daily players, that translates to EUR 200,000-600,000+ annually in F&B revenue.

As padel business consultant Manuel Romaguera noted in Padel Business Magazine: "A sustainable padel business begins by understanding the lifestyle, expectations and behaviour of the people you want to attract. Courts are the centrepiece, but the value is generated in everything that surrounds them" (Padel Business Magazine, December 2025).

### 5. Pro Shop and Equipment Retail (3-8% of revenue)

Rackets, balls, shoes, grips, bags, and apparel. Margins on padel equipment typically run 30-50%. A well-stocked pro shop in a busy club can generate EUR 30,000-100,000 in annual sales. Racket demo programs and partnerships with brands like Bullpadel, Head, Wilson, or Nox can provide additional margin through wholesale arrangements.

### 6. Events, Tournaments, and Corporate Hire (5-10% of revenue)

Social tournaments, corporate team-building events, birthday parties, and league play create incremental revenue while building community. Corporate bookings command premium rates -- typically 50-100% above standard court rental -- and often include F&B packages. A club running 2-4 events per month can generate EUR 20,000-80,000 in additional annual revenue.

### 7. Ancillary Revenue

Smaller but meaningful streams include:
- **Locker rental**: EUR 5-15/month per locker
- **Equipment rental**: Racket hire at EUR 3-5 per session
- **Sponsorship and advertising**: Court-side advertising, branded events
- **Video/streaming services**: Match recording and analysis (some clubs charge EUR 5-10 per match for automated video)

## Operating Costs

Understanding the cost structure is critical for realistic profitability projections.

### Major Cost Categories (% of revenue)

- **Rent/mortgage**: 15-30% (the single largest variable; location-dependent)
- **Staff**: 20-30% (reception, maintenance, coaching staff, management)
- **Utilities**: 5-10% (lighting, HVAC for indoor facilities, water)
- **Court maintenance**: 3-5% (turf replacement every 5-7 years at EUR 3,000-6,000 per court, glass cleaning, structural upkeep)
- **Technology/software**: 2-4% (booking platform fees, POS systems, website)
- **Marketing**: 3-7% (digital marketing, local advertising, events)
- **Insurance**: 1-3%
- **Consumables and supplies**: 2-3%

### Playtomic and Booking Platform Fees

Most clubs use booking platforms, and Playtomic dominates the market with its matchmaking and court management features. However, platform fees are a real cost. Some operators report paying over $5,000 annually in Playtomic fees, particularly for skill-based match bookings facilitated through the app (Reddit, July 2025). Clubs must weigh the customer acquisition benefits against the commission cost -- typically 10-20% of bookings made through the platform.

Playtomic reported EUR 346 million transacted through its platform as of September 2025, growing 51% year-over-year (Crowdcube, November 2025). The platform serves as both a booking tool and a player acquisition channel, making it nearly essential for new clubs but a cost that mature clubs with strong direct bookings may seek to minimize.

### Typical P&L for a 6-Court Club

A well-run 6-court club in a mid-tier European market might look like this:

**Annual Revenue**: EUR 400,000-600,000
- Court rentals: EUR 250,000-350,000
- Coaching: EUR 40,000-80,000
- Memberships: EUR 30,000-60,000
- F&B: EUR 50,000-80,000
- Pro shop and other: EUR 30,000-50,000

**Annual Operating Costs**: EUR 280,000-400,000
- Rent: EUR 80,000-150,000
- Staff: EUR 80,000-120,000
- Utilities: EUR 25,000-50,000
- Maintenance: EUR 15,000-25,000
- Marketing/technology: EUR 20,000-35,000
- Other: EUR 15,000-25,000

**EBITDA**: EUR 100,000-200,000 (25-35% margin)

## ROI and Breakeven Timeline

The breakeven question is the one every prospective operator asks first, and the answer depends heavily on initial investment, rent structure, and local demand.

**Operational breakeven** -- the point at which monthly revenue covers monthly operating costs -- is typically achievable within 12-18 months for a well-located club with effective pre-launch marketing and community building. Rex's Operator Guide suggests most clubs aim for 18-30 months (Rex, 2025).

**Full capital recovery** takes longer. Financial Models Lab projects operational breakeven around 14 months but notes that full return on initial capital investment can take 56 months (roughly 4.5 years) for a comprehensive padel center (Financial Models Lab, December 2025).

Key variables affecting ROI:
- **Location quality**: Proximity to residential density, commuter access, parking
- **Court utilization**: Every 10% improvement in utilization dramatically impacts profitability
- **Rent structure**: Clubs that own their real estate have fundamentally different economics than those leasing
- **Seasonality**: Indoor facilities in northern climates have more consistent year-round revenue
- **Ancillary revenue development**: Clubs that invest in F&B, coaching programs, and events break even faster

## Franchise Models

The franchise model is gaining traction as a way to scale padel operations while reducing risk for individual operators.

### Conquer Padel (U.S.)

The most prominent padel franchise in the United States. Conquer Padel opened its flagship club in Tempe, Arizona in 2025 and announced plans for 50 locations within a year. The company has signed franchisees in New Jersey and Orlando and raised $15 million in a Reg D 506(c) private equity offering with a minimum investment of $50,000 (BusinessWire, September 2025; March 2025). Conquer positions itself as a turnkey franchise model combining real estate, sport, and lifestyle.

### P1 Padel (U.S.)

Described as the first registered padel franchise in the United States, P1 Padel focuses on replicating a club model that combines sport with social experience (Interlink FBC, October 2025).

### Padelcreations (Europe)

A European court manufacturer and consultancy that offers end-to-end club development services, including business planning, court construction, and operational support. Their model is less a traditional franchise and more a turnkey development partnership (Padelcreations, 2025).

### European Chain Operators

In Spain, operators like Padel Nuestro have built networks combining retail, coaching, and club operations. In the UK, Game4Padel and We Are Padel have scaled multi-location operations. In Sweden, Padel Zenter and others have built substantial court networks leveraging the country's high per-capita padel adoption.

## Success Patterns and Failure Modes

### What Works

**Community-first operators**: Clubs that invest in community building -- leagues, social events, coaching ladders, beginner programs -- achieve higher retention and utilization. Padel's social DNA rewards operators who understand they are running a community hub, not just renting courts.

**Multi-revenue operators**: Clubs that layer F&B, coaching, retail, and events on top of court rental consistently outperform single-stream operators. The Los Naranjos model in Marbella -- treating the club as a hospitality experience comparable to a spa or golf club -- represents the premium end of this approach.

**Technology adopters**: Clubs that leverage platforms like Playtomic for matchmaking and booking, automated court lighting, and data-driven pricing (dynamic peak/off-peak rates) operate more efficiently and attract more players.

**Location discipline**: The three most important factors in padel club success remain location, location, and location. Proximity to dense residential areas with high disposable income is the single strongest predictor of utilization.

### What Fails

**Build-it-and-they-will-come**: Operators who invest heavily in court construction without corresponding investment in community development, marketing, and programming frequently face disappointing utilization rates.

**Underestimating working capital**: Many club failures stem not from poor demand but from insufficient capital to weather the 12-18 month ramp-up period before reaching operational breakeven.

**Outdoor-only in seasonal climates**: Clubs in northern Europe or northern U.S. states that build outdoor-only facilities face 3-5 months of minimal revenue annually. The math rarely works without an indoor component.

**Oversaturated locations**: In parts of Spain and Italy, the pace of court construction has outrun local demand growth. Opening the fifth padel club within a 5km radius requires a differentiated value proposition.

**Neglecting F&B**: Courts alone do not create a sticky customer experience. Clubs without comfortable lounge areas and food-and-beverage service miss a significant revenue opportunity and reduce the "hang time" that builds community.

## Club Software and Booking Platforms

The technology layer supporting padel club operations has matured rapidly:

- **Playtomic**: The dominant platform globally, serving thousands of clubs across 30+ countries. Offers court booking, matchmaking, club management, and payment processing. Rafael Nadal serves as brand ambassador. The platform raised $70 million in March 2025 (Sports Business Journal).
- **Rex**: A booking and member management platform positioning itself as an operator-friendly alternative with lower commission structures.
- **MATCHi**: A Swedish-origin platform strong in Scandinavian markets, offering booking, matchmaking, and league management.
- **Kourts**: A newer entrant focused on the U.S. market with padel-specific features.

The choice of booking platform affects both customer acquisition and operating costs. Playtomic's massive user base (estimated at several million active users) provides a powerful customer acquisition channel, but clubs should carefully model the fee impact on their unit economics.

## The Verdict for Prospective Operators

Padel club economics are favorable compared to most sports facility businesses, but they are not the guaranteed goldmine that promotional materials sometimes suggest. The fundamental equation works: strong demand growth, high repeat visit rates, compact real estate requirements, and multiple revenue opportunities create a genuinely attractive business model.

The operators who will thrive in 2026 and beyond are those who approach padel as a hospitality and community business that happens to have courts, rather than a court rental business that happens to serve coffee. The sport's social DNA is its greatest commercial asset -- operators who amplify that social experience will capture disproportionate value as the market continues to grow.

For anyone serious about entering the padel business, the advice is consistent across every successful operator interviewed: invest in community, plan for 18-24 months of ramp-up, diversify your revenue from day one, and never underestimate the importance of a good kitchen.
