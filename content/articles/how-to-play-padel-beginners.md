# How to Play Padel: A Beginner's Complete Guide

You have heard about padel, booked a court, and now you want to know what to actually do when you get there. This guide covers everything a beginner needs: the fundamental techniques, basic positioning, strategic principles, on-court etiquette, what to bring, and how to find courts and playing partners. By the end, you will have a clear roadmap for your first sessions and beyond.

## Before You Step on Court

### What to Bring

You need less gear for padel than you might expect.

**A padel racket.** If you are just starting, you do not need to invest heavily. Many padel facilities rent rackets for a few dollars per session, and this is perfectly fine for your first several games. If you want to buy your own, look for a round-shaped racket in the 50 to 120 dollar range -- these offer the largest sweet spot and the most forgiveness. Brands like Head, Babolat, Bullpadel, and Nox all make excellent beginner models.

**Padel balls.** These look similar to tennis balls but have slightly less internal pressure, making them slower. A can of three costs a few dollars. Your facility may provide them, but it is worth having your own.

**Court shoes.** Wear shoes designed for court sports -- tennis shoes or indoor court shoes with flat, non-marking soles and good lateral support. Avoid running shoes, which have thick heels and curved soles that can cause ankle rolls during the quick lateral movements padel demands. Avoid any shoe with aggressive tread patterns that could damage the artificial turf surface.

**Comfortable clothing.** Standard athletic wear is fine. Shorts or skirts, a breathable top, and sport socks. No special padel-specific clothing is required.

**Water.** Padel is more physically demanding than it looks. Stay hydrated.

### The Grip

Before anything else, you need to hold the racket correctly. The standard padel grip is the continental grip -- the same grip used for volleys in tennis.

To find it, hold the racket perpendicular to the ground as if you were shaking someone's hand. Your index finger's base knuckle should sit on the first bevel of the handle. Another way to find it: hold the racket by the face with your non-dominant hand, then let your dominant hand slide down to the handle naturally. Where it lands is approximately the continental grip.

This single grip works for nearly every shot in padel. Unlike tennis, where players constantly switch between forehand and backhand grips, padel's continental grip allows quick transitions between different shots -- essential in a fast-paced doubles game played on a small court.

Use a relaxed hold. Gripping the racket too tightly reduces wrist flexibility and causes early fatigue. Tighten your grip only at the moment of impact.

## The Fundamental Shots

### The Serve

The serve is underarm in padel, making it the most accessible element of the game.

**How to execute it:**

1. Stand behind the service line on the right side of the court (for the first point)
2. Hold the ball in your non-racket hand
3. Bounce the ball on the ground in front of you
4. As it rises from the bounce, strike it with the racket at or below waist height
5. Aim diagonally into the opponent's service box

Keep the serve simple at first. Focus on getting it in rather than hitting it hard. A reliable, deep serve that lands near the back of the service box is far more valuable than a fast serve that misses.

Common beginner mistakes on the serve: hitting the ball too high (above waist height, which is a fault), not bouncing the ball before hitting it, and aiming too aggressively. Start conservative and develop consistency before adding pace or placement.

After serving, move toward the net immediately. The serving team wants to establish a net position as quickly as possible -- this is one of padel's most fundamental strategic principles.

### The Forehand

The padel forehand is shorter and more compact than a tennis forehand. The smaller court and faster reaction times do not allow for the long, looping backswings common in tennis.

**Key points:**

- Use the continental grip (same grip as all other shots)
- Prepare early by turning your shoulders and taking the racket back
- Keep the backswing short -- the racket should not go much past your back shoulder
- Strike the ball in front of your body with a slightly open racket face
- Follow through toward your target

At the beginner level, focus on consistency over power. Hit the ball with a firm but controlled swing, aiming to keep rallies going. Power will develop naturally as your timing improves.

### The Backhand

Most beginners find the backhand challenging at first. In padel, you can use either a one-handed or two-handed backhand, and both are common.

**One-handed backhand:** Offers more reach, especially useful when the ball comes to you wide or when you are stretched. Keep the wrist firm and drive through the ball.

**Two-handed backhand:** Provides more stability and control, particularly for beginners. The non-dominant hand supports the racket through the swing, helping maintain a consistent racket face angle.

Regardless of which style you choose, the principles are the same: short backswing, contact in front of the body, and follow through toward the target. Start with the two-handed version if you are unsure -- it is more forgiving.

### The Volley

The volley is arguably the most important shot in padel. Since the net position is the dominant position in padel strategy, you will spend much of your time volleying.

**Key points:**

- Step forward into the volley, transferring weight toward the net
- Keep the racket out in front of you, ready to react
- Use a short, punching motion rather than a full swing
- Aim for placement over power -- direct your volleys toward the opponents' feet or into open space
- Keep the racket face slightly open to create a gentle upward trajectory over the net

The split step is essential. As your opponent is about to hit the ball, take a small hop and land with your feet shoulder-width apart, knees slightly bent. This puts you in a ready position to move in any direction. Professional padel players split step before virtually every shot.

### Wall Shots

Wall play is what makes padel unique, and it is the aspect that takes the longest to develop. Do not expect to master it quickly -- give yourself time.

**Playing the ball off the back wall:**

When a deep shot bounces on the floor and then hits the back glass, resist the urge to swing at it immediately. Instead:

1. Turn and face the back wall
2. Watch the ball come off the glass
3. Let it drop to a comfortable hitting height
4. Play a controlled shot back toward the net

The most common beginner error with wall shots is standing too close to the glass. Give yourself space -- at least an arm's length from the back wall -- so you have room to swing.

**Playing the ball off the side wall:**

Side wall shots often involve the ball changing direction unexpectedly. The key is to watch the ball all the way and adjust your position continuously. With practice, you will start reading the angles instinctively.

**A critical tip:** Do not try to hit wall shots hard. Focus on getting the ball back over the net with control. A soft, high return to the center of the opponents' court is infinitely better than an aggressive shot that hits the net.

## Positioning and Movement

### The Two Positions

Padel positioning revolves around two zones:

**The net (attacking position):** Both players stand approximately 2 to 3 meters from the net, side by side, each covering half the court. From here, you can volley, play overhead smashes, and put pressure on the opponents. This is where you want to be.

**The back (defensive position):** Both players stand near the back wall, ready to retrieve deep shots and lobs. From here, the goal is to transition back to the net through lobs, well-placed groundstrokes, or forcing errors.

### Moving as a Pair

In padel, you and your partner move together as a unit, connected by an invisible rope approximately 3 to 4 meters long. When your partner moves left, you move left. When they move forward, you move forward. This coordinated movement prevents gaps in your coverage and ensures that the opponents cannot exploit open space.

Think of the court as split down the middle. Each player is responsible for their half. The center of the court is shared territory, and clear communication ("mine," "yours") prevents confusion on balls that land in the middle.

### Transitioning Between Positions

Moving from the back to the net is the single most important tactical movement in padel. You transition forward after hitting a shot that puts the opponents under pressure -- typically a deep lob, a low ball that forces them to hit upward, or a well-placed shot to the feet.

Do not rush the transition. Move forward together, step by step, following your shot. If you hit a good ball, take two or three steps forward. If the opponents hit a strong reply, hold your position or retreat. Patience is the key.

## Basic Strategy

### Control the Net

The team at the net wins most points in padel. This is the single most important strategic concept. Every shot you hit should be chosen with the goal of either maintaining your net position or working your way to it.

### Lob Effectively

The lob is padel's most important tactical weapon. A well-placed lob over the opponents' heads forces them to retreat from the net, potentially giving you the opportunity to move forward and take the attacking position. Aim your lobs deep, landing them near the back glass. High, deep lobs are difficult to attack and give you time to reposition.

### Play to the Center

Hitting to the center of the court between the two opponents creates confusion about who should take the ball. It also reduces the angles available for their return. When in doubt, aim for the center.

### Be Patient

Padel rewards patience. Unlike tennis, where a powerful shot can end the point outright, padel's walls keep the ball alive. Trying to force winners often leads to errors. Instead, build the point gradually: hit to good positions, wait for a short ball or a weak reply, and then look to finish.

### Reduce Unforced Errors

At the beginner level, most points are lost rather than won. The player or pair that makes fewer mistakes will win the majority of games. Prioritize consistency and placement over power and flair.

## Etiquette

Padel culture places a high value on sportsmanship and social grace. Here are the conventions every player should follow.

### Before the Match

- Arrive on time. Court time is booked by the hour, and late arrivals cut into everyone's playing time.
- Warm up together. Spend the first five minutes hitting cooperatively with all four players, getting the ball in play and warming up your muscles and reflexes.

### During the Match

- Call the ball. When a ball lands near you and your partner, communicate clearly about who will take it.
- Call "out" clearly and immediately if a ball lands outside the lines on your side. Be honest -- in social padel, integrity matters more than winning.
- Do not celebrate excessively or react negatively to your own errors. Keep your composure.
- Acknowledge good shots from your opponents with a nod or a brief word. This is standard practice in padel culture.
- Collect balls efficiently between points. Do not leave balls lying on the court -- they are a tripping hazard.

### After the Match

- Shake hands (or tap rackets) with all players at the net
- The losing pair traditionally handles the court reservation or buys the first round of drinks -- customs vary by region, but the spirit of generosity is universal

### General Court Conduct

- Do not walk behind other courts while a point is in play
- Keep your phone on silent and off the court
- Respect your booked time -- start on time, finish on time
- Return rental equipment in good condition

## Finding Courts and Playing Partners

### Court Booking Apps

**Playtomic** is the most widely used padel court booking platform, available globally. It allows you to search for nearby courts, see availability, book in real time, and even find partners to play with. Other regional platforms include Matchi, Padel Manager, and various club-specific apps.

### Americano Sessions

Many padel facilities organize "Americano" events -- social round-robin tournaments where players rotate partners and opponents throughout the session. These are the single best way to meet other players, improve your game through varied competition, and become part of a local padel community. They typically cost the price of court time plus a small organizational fee.

### Lessons and Clinics

Investing in a few lessons as a beginner pays dividends. A qualified coach can correct grip issues, teach you how to read the ball off the walls, and introduce positioning concepts that would take months to discover on your own. Many clubs offer group clinics at affordable rates, and the social element of group learning adds to the experience.

### Local Groups and Communities

Search social media platforms for local padel groups. Facebook groups, WhatsApp communities, and dedicated padel forums are active in most cities where the sport has a presence. These groups organize casual games, share court availability, and welcome newcomers.

## Your First Session: What to Expect

Your first time on a padel court will feel slightly chaotic, and that is completely normal. Here is a realistic expectation:

- The walls will confuse you at first. Balls will bounce off the glass at angles you did not expect, and you will miss shots you thought were easy. This is universal -- every padel player went through the same adjustment.
- You will discover that the underarm serve is easier than you expected. Most beginners can get the serve in by their third or fourth attempt.
- Rallies will be shorter than you hope. As you improve, they will get longer.
- You will find yourself laughing. Padel has a unique ability to generate enjoyable moments even when you are making mistakes.
- You will leave the court wanting to play again.

Padel hooks people quickly. The combination of physical activity, tactical problem-solving, social interaction, and the sheer novelty of wall play creates an experience that stays with you. Most people who try padel once become regular players within weeks.

## Progressing Beyond Beginner

Once you have the basics down and can sustain rallies, maintain a net position, and execute the fundamental shots with reasonable consistency, you are ready to begin developing intermediate skills:

- **The bandeja:** An overhead shot played with slice, used as a tactical reset rather than a power shot. It is the bread-and-butter overhead in padel.
- **The vibora:** A side-spin overhead that kicks off the side glass, making it difficult for opponents to return.
- **The chiquita:** A soft, low shot aimed at the opponents' feet, designed to force a weak return and maintain net position.
- **The bajada:** An aggressive overhead played off a high ball that bounces off the back wall, turning defense into attack.

These shots separate beginner padel from intermediate play, and each one builds on the fundamental techniques covered in this guide.

## Final Thoughts

Padel is a sport that meets you where you are. It asks for nothing more than a willingness to show up, swing a racket, and share a court with three other people. The learning curve is gentle enough that your first session will be enjoyable, and the strategic depth is rich enough that your thousandth session will still teach you something new.

Grab a racket, find a court, and bring three friends. The rest will take care of itself.
