# How to Play Padel: A Beginner's Complete Guide

You've booked a court. You're showing up in an hour. Here's everything you actually need to know before you walk on.

## What to bring

Less than you'd think.

**A padel racket.** If you're starting out, don't invest. Most facilities rent rackets for a few euros per session, which is fine for your first several games. If you're buying, look for a round-shaped racket in the €50–€120 range. Round shapes have the largest sweet spot and the most forgiveness. Head, Babolat, Bullpadel, and Nox all make solid beginner models.

**Padel balls.** They look like tennis balls but with slightly less internal pressure, which makes them slower. A can of three is a few euros. Your facility may provide them, but it's worth having your own.

**Court shoes.** Tennis shoes or indoor court shoes with flat, non-marking soles. Avoid running shoes; their thick heels and curved soles cause ankle rolls during the lateral movements padel demands. Avoid aggressive tread patterns that damage artificial turf.

**Comfortable clothing.** Standard athletic wear. Shorts or skirts, breathable top, sport socks. No padel-specific gear required.

**Water.** Padel is more physically demanding than it looks. Hydrate.

## The grip

Before anything else, hold the racket correctly. Padel uses the **continental grip**, the same grip used for volleys in tennis.

To find it: hold the racket perpendicular to the ground as if you were shaking someone's hand. Your index finger's base knuckle should sit on the first bevel of the handle. Or: hold the racket by the face with your non-dominant hand, then let your dominant hand slide down the handle naturally. Where it lands is approximately the continental grip.

This single grip works for nearly every shot in padel. Unlike tennis, where players constantly switch between forehand and backhand grips, the continental grip lets you transition fast between shots in a fast-paced game on a small court.

Keep a relaxed hold. Gripping the racket too tightly kills your wrist flexibility and tires your forearm out. Tighten only at the moment of impact.

## The serve

Underarm. The most accessible element of the game.

How to do it:

1. Stand behind the service line on the right (for the first point of the game).
2. Hold the ball in your non-racket hand.
3. Bounce the ball on the ground in front of you.
4. As it rises from the bounce, strike it with the racket at or below waist height.
5. Aim diagonally into the opponents' service box.

Keep it simple at first. Get it in. A reliable, deep serve is far more valuable than a fast one that misses. After serving, move forward toward the net immediately. The serving team wants the net position as quickly as possible; this is one of padel's most fundamental tactical principles.

Common beginner mistakes: hitting the ball above waist height (a fault), forgetting to bounce the ball first, aiming too aggressively. Start conservative.

## The forehand

The padel forehand is shorter and more compact than a tennis forehand. The smaller court and faster reaction times don't allow for the long, looping backswings common in tennis.

Key points:

- Continental grip (same as everything else)
- Prepare early: turn shoulders, take racket back
- Keep the backswing short. The racket should not go much past your back shoulder.
- Strike the ball in front of your body, racket face slightly open
- Follow through toward your target

At the beginner level, focus on consistency, not power. Hit firm and controlled, just keep rallies going. Power develops naturally as your timing improves.

## The backhand

Most beginners find the backhand harder. You can use either a one-handed or two-handed version; both are common in padel.

**One-handed.** More reach, especially when the ball comes wide or you're stretched. Keep the wrist firm and drive through the ball.

**Two-handed.** More stability and control, more forgiving for beginners. The non-dominant hand supports the racket through the swing, holding the racket face angle steady.

Either way: short backswing, contact in front of the body, follow through toward the target. Start two-handed if you're unsure.

## The volley

The most important shot in padel. Since the net position is the dominant attacking position, you'll spend much of your time volleying.

- Step forward into the volley, transferring weight toward the net.
- Keep the racket out in front of you, ready to react.
- Use a short, punching motion rather than a full swing.
- Aim for placement, not power. Direct your volleys at the opponents' feet or into open space.
- Keep the racket face slightly open to give the ball a gentle upward trajectory over the net.

The **split step** is essential. As your opponent is about to hit, take a small hop and land with feet shoulder-width apart, knees slightly bent. That puts you ready to move in any direction. Pros split-step before virtually every shot.

## Wall shots

Wall play is what makes padel unique, and it takes the longest to develop. Don't expect to master it quickly. Give yourself time.

**Off the back wall.** When a deep shot bounces on the floor and then hits the back glass, resist the urge to swing immediately. Instead:

1. Turn and face the back wall
2. Watch the ball come off the glass
3. Let it drop to a comfortable hitting height
4. Play a controlled shot back toward the net

The most common beginner error: standing too close to the glass. Give yourself space, at least an arm's length from the back wall, so you have room to swing.

**Off the side wall.** Side wall shots often involve the ball changing direction unpredictably. Watch the ball all the way and adjust your position continuously. With practice you'll start reading the angles instinctively.

**The most important rule for wall shots:** don't try to hit them hard. Focus on getting the ball back over the net with control. A soft, high return to the centre of the opponents' court is infinitely better than an aggressive shot into the net.

## Positioning and movement

### The two positions

Padel has essentially two zones:

- **The net (attacking).** Both players stand 2–3 m from the net, side by side, each covering half the court. From here you volley, smash, and put pressure on the opponents. This is where you want to be.
- **The back (defending).** Both players stand near the back wall, ready to retrieve deep shots and lobs. From here, the goal is to transition back to the net through lobs, well-placed groundstrokes, or forcing errors.

### Move as a pair

You and your partner move together as a unit, connected by an invisible rope roughly 3–4 m long. Partner moves left, you move left. Partner moves forward, you move forward. Coordinated movement prevents gaps that opponents can exploit.

Think of the court as split down the middle. Each player covers their half. The centre is shared territory, and clear communication ("mine," "yours") prevents confusion on balls that land in the middle.

### Transitioning forward

Moving from back to net is the single most important tactical movement in padel. You transition forward after a shot that puts the opponents under pressure: a deep lob, a low ball that forces them to hit upward, or a well-placed shot to the feet.

Don't rush. Move forward together, step by step, following your shot. If you hit a good ball, take two or three steps forward. If the opponents hit a strong reply, hold position or retreat. Patience.

## Basic strategy

**Control the net.** The team at the net wins most points in padel. The single most important strategic concept. Every shot you play should be chosen with the goal of either keeping your net position or working your way to it.

**Lob effectively.** The lob is padel's most important tactical weapon. A well-placed lob over the opponents' heads forces them to retreat from the net, potentially giving you the opportunity to move forward into the attacking position. Aim deep, near the back glass. High and deep is hard to attack and gives you time to reposition.

**Play to the centre.** Hitting between the two opponents creates confusion about who should take the ball. It also reduces the angles available for their return. When in doubt, aim for the centre.

**Be patient.** Padel rewards patience. Unlike tennis, where a powerful shot can end the point outright, padel's walls keep the ball alive. Trying to force winners leads to errors. Build the point: hit to good positions, wait for a short ball or a weak reply, then look to finish.

**Reduce unforced errors.** At the beginner level, most points are lost rather than won. The pair that makes fewer mistakes wins the majority of games. Prioritise consistency and placement over power and flair.

## Etiquette

Padel culture places a high value on sportsmanship and social grace. The conventions every player should follow:

### Before the match

- Arrive on time. Court time is booked by the hour and late arrivals cut into everyone's playing time.
- Warm up together. Spend the first five minutes hitting cooperatively with all four players.

### During

- Call the ball: communicate clearly with your partner about who will take it.
- Call "out" clearly and immediately if a ball lands outside the lines on your side. In social padel, integrity matters more than winning.
- Don't celebrate excessively or react negatively to your own errors. Keep your composure.
- Acknowledge good shots from opponents with a nod or a brief word. Standard practice in padel.
- Collect balls efficiently between points. Don't leave them lying on the court; they're a tripping hazard.

### After

- Shake hands (or tap rackets) with all players at the net.
- The losing pair traditionally handles the court reservation or buys the first round of drinks. Customs vary by region; the spirit is universal.

### General

- Don't walk behind other courts while a point is in play.
- Phone on silent, off the court.
- Respect your booked time. Start on time, finish on time.
- Return rental equipment in good condition.

## Finding courts and partners

### Court-booking apps

**Playtomic** is the most widely used padel court booking platform, available globally. Search for nearby courts, see availability, book in real time, and find partners. Regional alternatives include Matchi, Padel Manager, and various club-specific apps.

### Americano sessions

Many facilities organise "Americano" events: social round-robin tournaments where players rotate partners and opponents throughout the session. The single best way to meet other players, improve your game through varied competition, and become part of a local padel community. Cost is usually court time plus a small organisational fee.

### Lessons and clinics

Even one or two lessons as a beginner pay outsized dividends. A coach can correct grip issues, teach you how to read the ball off the walls, and introduce positioning concepts that take months to discover on your own. Many clubs run group clinics at affordable rates.

### Local communities

Search social media for local padel groups. Facebook, WhatsApp, and dedicated padel forums are active in most cities where the sport has a presence. These groups organise casual games, share court availability, and welcome newcomers.

## Your first session: what to expect

Your first time will feel slightly chaotic. That's normal.

- The walls will confuse you at first. Balls will bounce off the glass at angles you didn't expect, and you'll miss shots you thought were easy. Universal experience; every padel player goes through it.
- The underarm serve is easier than you expected. Most beginners can get the serve in by their third or fourth attempt.
- Rallies will be shorter than you hope. As you improve, they get longer.
- You'll find yourself laughing. Padel has an unusual ability to generate enjoyment even when you're making mistakes.
- You'll leave wanting to play again.

Padel hooks people quickly. The combination of physical activity, tactical problem-solving, social interaction, and the novelty of wall play creates an experience that stays with you. Most people who try it once become regular players within weeks.

## Once you're past beginner

Once you can sustain rallies, hold a net position, and execute the fundamental shots with reasonable consistency, you're ready for the intermediate vocabulary:

- **Bandeja.** An overhead played with slice, used as a tactical reset rather than a power shot. The bread-and-butter overhead in padel.
- **Víbora.** A side-spin overhead that kicks off the side glass and makes it difficult to return.
- **Chiquita.** A soft, low shot aimed at the opponents' feet, designed to force a weak return and let you keep the net position.
- **Bajada.** An aggressive overhead played off a high ball that bounces off the back wall, turning defence into attack.

These four shots separate beginner padel from intermediate play. Each builds on the fundamentals in this guide.

That's the whole starter kit. Grab a racket, find a court, bring three friends. The rest takes care of itself.
