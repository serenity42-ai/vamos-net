# The Business of Padel: Market Size, Investment, and Growth in 2026

A sport invented in a Mexican backyard in 1969 is now a six-billion-euro industry. The court construction market alone is half a billion dollars annually. Qatar Sports Investments — the same fund that owns Paris Saint-Germain — backs the global tour. Private equity is pouring into court chains. Saudi Arabia is hosting tournaments as part of Vision 2030. The numbers are real, the capital is institutional, and the runway is long.

Here is how the money actually works.

## Global market size

### The numbers

The padel industry's total market value depends on how broadly you define it. Multiple market research reports paint a consistent picture of an industry in rapid expansion:

- The **Global Padel Report** projects the total padel market to reach approximately **€6 billion in 2026**, tripling from an estimated €2 billion in 2022
- The global **padel sports market** (equipment, apparel, accessories) was valued at approximately **$274 million in 2026**, up from $248 million in 2025, according to Global Growth Insights
- The **padel court construction market** alone is estimated at approximately **$500 million in 2026**
- Projections indicate the broader padel sports market could reach **$660 million by 2035**, with a CAGR of approximately 10.3%

These figures capture different slices. The €6 billion figure encompasses everything from court bookings and club memberships to equipment sales, professional tour revenues, broadcasting rights, and ancillary services. The narrower research figures focus on specific segments.

### Participation numbers

The financial growth is driven by staggering participation increases:

- **35 million active padel players** worldwide as of 2025–2026
- **150+ countries** with organised padel activity
- **70,000+ padel courts** globally, projected to represent a 40% increase from 2024 levels
- Spain alone has approximately **25,000 courts** and over 6 million players
- Italy has emerged as one of the fastest-growing markets, adding thousands of courts annually
- The UK, Germany, France, and the Nordic countries are experiencing double-digit annual growth

## The court construction boom

### Building the infrastructure

The most visible sign of padel's business growth is the global court construction boom. New courts are being built at an extraordinary rate across Europe, the Middle East, Asia, and increasingly in North America and Oceania.

**Cost of building a padel court:**

- Basic outdoor court (concrete walls, no glass): €15,000–€25,000
- Standard court (glass walls, artificial turf): €30,000–€50,000
- Premium panoramic court (full glass, professional grade): €50,000–€80,000
- Indoor court (including building structure): €100,000–€200,000+ per court

**Leading court manufacturers** include Mondo, Manzasport, Padelcreations, and several other European firms. Demand has driven manufacturers to expand production capacity, with some reporting order backlogs of 6–12 months.

### Real estate play

Padel courts are increasingly attractive to real estate developers and property companies:

- A single padel court requires only **200 m²** of space (less than half a tennis court)
- Return on investment for a well-located padel court can be achieved in 2–5 years
- Courts can be installed on rooftops, parking structures, and underutilised urban lots
- Premium urban locations command booking rates of €40–€80 per hour, generating strong revenue per square metre
- Indoor padel centres with 4–8 courts have become a popular format for commercial real estate investors

### The multi-court club model

The economics of padel favour multi-court facilities:

- **4-court minimum** is the threshold for a viable standalone padel business
- **8–12 court complexes** are the sweet spot for dedicated padel centres
- Additional revenue streams include coaching, equipment retail, food and beverage, and event hosting
- **Club membership models** provide predictable recurring revenue
- **Peak-hour booking premiums** (evenings and weekends) can be 2–3× off-peak rates

## The professional tour economy

### Premier Padel

The launch of **Premier Padel** in 2022, backed by the FIP and **Qatar Sports Investments (QSI)**, transformed the professional side of the business. QSI — the same entity behind Paris Saint-Germain football club — brought institutional-level investment and commercial sophistication to padel.

Key business elements of the Premier Padel structure:

**Title sponsorship.** Qatar Airways serves as the title sponsor of the 2026 tour, lending global brand recognition and significant financial support.

**Prize money.** Premier Padel has significantly increased prize money compared to the former World Padel Tour, attracting top players and raising the professional standards of the sport.

**Tournament hosting fees.** Cities and countries bid to host Premier Padel events, paying hosting fees in exchange for the tourism, media exposure, and prestige associated with a major sporting event. The 2026 season opener in Riyadh, Saudi Arabia, reflects the tour's appeal to nations investing in sports tourism.

### Broadcast deals

Television and streaming rights are where the serious money is in professional sports, and padel is making rapid strides:

- **beIN Sports** secured broadcast rights for the 2026 Qatar Airways Premier Padel tour and the FIP World Cup across **39 territories**, spanning the Middle East, North Africa, Southeast Asia, Oceania, and North America
- **Red Bull TV** provides coverage of Premier Padel matches from quarterfinals onward
- The Premier Padel YouTube channel streams matches to regions without exclusive broadcast deals
- Multiple national broadcasters in Spain, Argentina, and other core markets carry Premier Padel events

The broadcast landscape is still evolving. As padel's audience grows, the value of these rights is expected to increase significantly. Industry observers compare padel's current position to where UFC or Formula E were in their early years of broadcast development: emerging properties with substantial upside.

### Player economics

The financial reality for professional padel players varies dramatically by ranking:

- **Top 10 pairs** earn substantial income through prize money, sponsorship deals, and endorsements. Elite players have contracts with equipment brands, shoe companies, and apparel sponsors worth six and seven figures annually.
- **Players ranked 20–50** can make a living from padel but with tighter margins. Prize money from lower-tier events may not cover travel and coaching costs alone.
- **Players outside the top 100** often need supplementary income from coaching, exhibitions, or other employment.

The gap between the top and the rest is narrowing as Premier Padel's increased prize pools and sponsorship market mature, but padel player compensation still trails significantly behind tennis, football, or basketball.

## Equipment market

### Rackets

The padel racket market is dominated by a mix of established sports brands and padel-first companies:

- **Major brands:** Adidas, Babolat, Head, Wilson, Nike (limited range)
- **Padel-first brands:** Bullpadel, Nox, StarVie, Siux, Varlion
- Premium rackets retail for €200–€350, with entry-level models at €50–€100
- The professional endorsement market is active, with top players commanding significant fees for using branded equipment
- Direct-to-consumer sales through online platforms are growing, challenging traditional retail distribution

### Shoes and apparel

Padel footwear and apparel represent a growing segment:

- **Asics** dominates padel shoe sales, with Babolat, Bullpadel, Joma, and Head also competing
- Padel-specific clothing lines are offered by most major padel brands
- The crossover between tennis and padel apparel is significant, with many players using tennis clothing

### Balls

Padel uses pressurised balls similar to tennis balls but with slightly lower pressure:

- **Head** is the official ball supplier for Premier Padel
- Ball consumption is high (a can per match at a minimum), creating a steady consumable revenue stream
- Major ball brands include Head, Bullpadel, Wilson, and Dunlop

## Technology and platforms

### Booking platforms

Digital booking platforms have become integral to the padel business:

- **Playtomic** is the dominant global platform, connecting players with courts in over 40 countries. The company has raised significant venture capital funding and is valued as one of the most successful sports technology companies in Europe.
- **Padel Manager** provides club management software including booking, membership management, and payment processing
- **Matchi** serves the Nordic market
- Various regional platforms serve specific markets

These platforms generate revenue through booking commissions (typically 5–15% of the booking fee), premium subscriptions for clubs, and data-driven services.

### Data and analytics

Professional padel is increasingly data-driven:

- Player tracking technology is being implemented at top-tier events
- Statistical analysis of shot patterns, court positioning, and match dynamics is growing
- Fantasy padel and sports betting on padel matches are emerging revenue streams

## Investment trends

### Who is investing

Padel has attracted capital from diverse sources:

- **Sovereign wealth funds and state-backed entities.** QSI's involvement in Premier Padel. Saudi Arabia's hosting of major events as part of its Vision 2030 sports strategy.
- **Private equity.** Several PE firms have invested in padel club chains, court manufacturers, and technology platforms.
- **Celebrity and athlete investors.** High-profile individuals from football, tennis, and other sports have invested in padel clubs and brands.
- **Real estate developers.** Integrating padel courts into residential and commercial developments as amenities.
- **Sports media groups.** Acquiring broadcast rights and investing in content production around padel.

### Regional investment hotspots

**Europe.** Spain remains the largest market, but Italy, the UK, France, Germany, and the Nordic countries are attracting the most new investment.

**Middle East.** Saudi Arabia, the UAE, and Qatar are investing heavily in padel infrastructure and event hosting. The climate (with indoor facilities) and affluent demographics make the region highly attractive.

**Asia.** Japan's hosting of the 2026 Asian Games with padel as an official sport is catalysing investment across the continent. South Korea, India, and Southeast Asian nations are beginning to build padel infrastructure.

**Americas.** While Argentina remains mature, the United States is widely seen as the largest untapped market. Several companies are targeting the US with indoor padel facilities in major metropolitan areas.

## Club economics: a closer look

### Revenue model for a typical 6-court padel club

**Revenue streams:**

- Court bookings: 55–65% of revenue
- Coaching and clinics: 15–20%
- Equipment sales (rackets, shoes, accessories): 5–10%
- Food and beverage: 5–10%
- Memberships and subscriptions: 5–10%
- Events and tournaments: 2–5%

**Key metrics:**

- Average court utilisation target: 60–75% during operating hours
- Peak booking rate (evening/weekend): €40–€80 per 90-minute session
- Off-peak rate: €20–€40 per session
- Annual revenue per court: €40,000–€80,000 (varies dramatically by location and utilisation)
- Operating margin: 15–30% for well-managed facilities

**Major cost items:**

- Rent or mortgage: typically the largest fixed cost
- Staff (reception, coaching, maintenance): 25–35% of revenue
- Utilities (lighting, climate control for indoor): 8–15%
- Maintenance (surface, walls, equipment): 5–10%
- Marketing and technology (booking platform fees): 5–8%

### Success factors

The clubs that thrive tend to share common characteristics:

- Prime urban locations with easy access and high foot traffic
- Strong community building through leagues, social events, and coaching programmes
- Professional management with clear financial targets and operational discipline
- Digital-first booking through platforms like Playtomic
- Diversified revenue beyond just court bookings

## Challenges and risks

Despite the boom, the padel business is not without risks:

**Oversupply in mature markets.** In parts of Spain and Argentina, the court-building boom risks creating more supply than demand can absorb, putting pressure on booking rates and utilisation.

**Construction costs rising.** Glass prices, steel costs, and labour shortages have increased court construction costs in many markets.

**Seasonality.** Outdoor-only facilities in temperate climates face significant revenue drops in winter months.

**Regulatory uncertainty.** Zoning laws, noise regulations, and planning permissions can delay or block court construction projects.

**Market consolidation.** As the industry matures, smaller independent clubs may struggle to compete with well-funded chains and multi-sport facilities.

## Looking ahead: 2027 and beyond

The padel industry's trajectory suggests continued strong growth through the rest of the decade:

- **Olympic inclusion** (potentially for Brisbane 2032) would catalyse a new wave of investment and participation
- **US market development** could add millions of players and billions in market value
- **Technology integration** (AI coaching, advanced analytics, virtual training) will create new revenue streams
- **Media rights escalation** as audiences grow and competition for content intensifies
- **Sustainability focus** with eco-friendly court materials and energy-efficient facilities gaining traction

A sport that was virtually unknown outside Spain and Argentina 15 years ago now commands billion-euro market valuations, global broadcast deals, and the attention of some of the world's most sophisticated investors. For entrepreneurs, investors, and anyone considering entering the padel business, the fundamentals are strong: growing participation, limited infrastructure relative to demand, and a product that generates genuine enthusiasm and repeat engagement. The window of opportunity remains wide open.
