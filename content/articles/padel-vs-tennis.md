# Padel vs Tennis: Key Differences, Similarities, and Which is Right for You

Tennis has been the dominant racket sport globally for over a century. Padel, invented in 1969 and now played by over 35 million people, is mounting the most serious challenge to that position any sport has managed. The two games share DNA -- identical scoring, similar court geometry, and a net dividing two opposing sides -- but the experience of playing them is fundamentally different.

This guide breaks down every meaningful comparison between padel and tennis: the courts, the equipment, the physical demands, the social dynamics, the cost, and the all-important question of which one you should try.

## Court Size and Design

The most immediately obvious difference between padel and tennis is the court.

A standard tennis court measures 23.77 meters long by 10.97 meters wide for doubles (78 feet by 36 feet). It is open on all sides, with space beyond the baselines and sidelines for players to chase down shots.

A padel court measures 20 meters long by 10 meters wide (66 feet by 33 feet) -- roughly 25 percent the total area. But the real distinction is the enclosure. Padel courts are surrounded by walls of glass and metal mesh fencing, standing 3 to 4 meters high. These walls are not just barriers; they are part of the playing surface. Balls can be played off the glass and mesh, adding an entirely new dimension to every rally.

The smaller footprint of a padel court has practical implications. Two padel courts can fit in the space of one tennis court, making padel facilities more economically viable in urban areas where land is expensive. Indoor padel facilities can operate in converted warehouses, retail spaces, or purpose-built structures with lower ceiling requirements than indoor tennis.

## The Racket

Tennis rackets are strung with synthetic or natural gut strings stretched across an oval frame, typically measuring 68 to 73 centimeters in total length. They are lightweight but generate enormous power through the trampoline effect of the string bed.

Padel rackets are solid -- no strings at all. Instead, they feature a perforated surface (drilled with holes) made from carbon fiber, fiberglass, or a combination of both, with a foam core (usually EVA or FOAM). They are shorter than tennis rackets, typically measuring 45 to 46 centimeters, and weigh between 340 and 390 grams.

The solid construction of padel rackets means less power generation compared to a tennis racket, which is deliberate. Padel's smaller court would make a strung racket overpowered -- balls would fly out of the enclosure on every hard shot. The solid racket creates a game centered on placement, spin, and touch rather than pure pace.

For beginners, the padel racket is arguably easier to handle. The shorter length reduces the learning curve for making clean contact, and the larger face relative to the racket size offers a generous sweet spot, especially in round-shaped beginner models.

## Scoring

Padel and tennis use an identical scoring system. Points progress through 15, 30, 40, and game, with deuce at 40-all and advantage required to win. Sets are first to six games with a two-game lead, tiebreaks at 6-6, and matches are best of three sets.

The one minor variation is padel's occasional use of the "golden point" rule, where deuce is decided by a single point rather than requiring a two-point margin. The receiving pair chooses which side the serve goes to. This format is used in some professional tournaments and many social settings to speed up play. Tennis has no widespread equivalent, though some exhibition formats have experimented with similar concepts.

## The Serve

In tennis, the serve is often the most dominant shot in the game. Players toss the ball overhead and strike it with full force, generating speeds that regularly exceed 200 kilometers per hour at the professional level. The serve can win points outright through aces, and developing a powerful serve requires years of technique refinement.

In padel, the serve is underarm. The ball must be bounced before striking, and contact must be made at or below waist height. This eliminates the serve as a weapon and makes it primarily a way to start the point. Even at the professional level, aces in padel are extremely rare.

This difference has profound implications for accessibility. In tennis, facing an experienced player's serve can be an uncomfortable or even intimidating experience for a beginner. In padel, the underarm serve ensures that every player, regardless of experience, can get the ball in play and participate in rallies from the first game.

## Physicality and Athletic Demands

Tennis is one of the most physically demanding sports in existence. Professional matches can last over five hours. Players cover significant distances, sprinting laterally and forward and backward across a large court. The one-handed or two-handed groundstrokes require substantial upper-body strength, and the overhead serve places considerable stress on the shoulder, elbow, and wrist.

Padel is physically demanding in a different way. The smaller court means less total distance covered, but the game involves frequent short sprints, changes of direction, and explosive movements at the net. The underarm serve and lighter racket reduce stress on the shoulder joint, making padel generally easier on the body than tennis.

The wall play in padel creates a unique physical dimension: players must read the ball off glass, adjust their positioning accordingly, and often hit shots from unusual angles or while moving backward toward the back wall. This develops spatial awareness and reflexes in ways that tennis does not.

For older players, those recovering from injuries, or anyone seeking a less physically taxing racket sport, padel offers a compelling alternative. Many former tennis players have transitioned to padel precisely because it allows them to continue competing at a high level with less physical wear and tear.

That said, elite padel is no walk in the park. Professional players are supremely fit athletes, and the pace of play at the top level is relentless. The sport is as demanding as you want to make it.

## Social Dynamics

This is where the two sports diverge most dramatically.

Tennis can be played as singles or doubles, but singles is the dominant format at both recreational and professional levels. Singles tennis is an inherently individual pursuit -- you against one opponent, no one to share the burden with.

Padel is almost exclusively doubles. Four players are on court every session, which changes the social equation entirely. You need a partner, you need opponents, and the dynamic between all four players shapes the experience. Post-match socializing -- a drink, a meal, a debrief of key points -- is baked into padel culture in a way that it rarely is in casual tennis.

This social dimension is consistently cited as one of the primary reasons people take up padel and, more importantly, keep playing. It is easier to convince three friends to play padel than to find one evenly matched tennis opponent. The doubles format also means that skill gaps between players are less decisive -- a weaker player paired with a stronger partner can still compete effectively and have fun.

## Learning Curve

Tennis has a notoriously steep learning curve. Developing a reliable serve alone can take months of practice. Groundstrokes require precise footwork, timing, and racket preparation. Many beginners spend their first several sessions hitting balls into the net or over the fence, struggling to sustain rallies.

Padel's learning curve is gentler, for several reasons:

- **The walls keep the ball in play.** Shots that would be winners or errors in tennis bounce off the glass and return into the court, extending rallies and giving players more chances to hit the ball.
- **The underarm serve is easy to learn.** Most beginners can execute a legal serve within minutes.
- **The shorter racket is more manageable.** The compact padel racket requires less precise technique to make solid contact.
- **The smaller court means less ground to cover.** Players feel more in control of the space.

Most padel beginners report enjoying competitive rallies within their first or second session. In tennis, reaching the same level of rally consistency typically takes considerably longer.

However, padel's ceiling is extremely high. Mastering wall play, developing the full range of shots (bandeja, vibora, bajada, chiquita), and learning the nuances of doubles positioning and communication takes years. The sport is easy to start but difficult to master -- arguably the ideal combination for any sport seeking mass adoption.

## Cost Comparison

### Equipment

A beginner padel racket costs between 40 and 120 dollars. A competitive intermediate racket runs 120 to 250 dollars, and premium pro-level rackets range from 250 to 450 dollars.

A beginner tennis racket falls in a similar range, but the ongoing cost of stringing adds up. Tennis rackets need restringing every few weeks to few months depending on play frequency, at a cost of 20 to 50 dollars per job. Padel rackets, being solid, require no stringing -- though they do eventually wear out and need replacement.

Padel balls cost slightly less than tennis balls and are consumed at a similar rate.

### Court Time

Court rental costs vary by location but padel courts are generally comparable to or slightly more expensive than tennis courts on a per-hour basis. However, the cost is split four ways in padel (since you always need four players), making the per-person cost roughly half that of a tennis singles match.

### Clothing and Shoes

Both sports require similar athletic wear. Court shoes designed for lateral movement are recommended for both. There is no meaningful cost difference.

### Overall

When you factor in court cost splitting, no stringing expenses, and generally less expensive rackets at the entry level, padel is typically cheaper to play regularly than tennis.

## Accessibility

Padel scores higher than tennis on nearly every measure of accessibility:

- **Age range:** Padel is played competitively by children as young as six and adults well into their seventies and eighties. The reduced physical demands compared to tennis make it viable across a wider age span.
- **Fitness level:** You do not need to be particularly fit to enjoy a social game of padel. The smaller court and doubles format reduce the physical floor for participation.
- **Prior experience:** Complete beginners can participate meaningfully from their first session. Tennis typically requires more baseline skill to enjoy a rally-based game.
- **Availability:** While tennis courts are still more widely available globally, padel courts are being built at a far faster rate. In Spain, Italy, and several other European countries, padel courts now outnumber tennis courts. In emerging markets, new padel facilities are opening weekly.

Tennis retains advantages in some areas. It is more widely taught in schools, has a deeper infrastructure for junior development in most countries, and offers a solo practice option (hitting against a wall or using a ball machine) that padel does not easily replicate.

## Which is Right for You?

There is no wrong answer here -- both are excellent sports. But your preferences may point you in one direction.

### Choose Padel If:

- You want a social sport that guarantees interaction with other players
- You are new to racket sports and want a gentle learning curve
- You prefer doubles to singles competition
- You have joint concerns or want a less physically punishing option
- You enjoy strategic, tactical play over raw power
- You want to be part of a rapidly growing community with fresh energy

### Choose Tennis If:

- You enjoy solo competition and individual accountability
- You want to develop a powerful serve and aggressive baseline game
- You prefer a sport with a longer history and deeper competitive infrastructure
- You enjoy the physical challenge of covering a large court
- You want the option to practice alone
- You are pursuing a path in competitive junior or collegiate athletics (where tennis infrastructure is more established)

### Or Play Both

A growing number of players enjoy both sports. The skills are complementary -- tennis players often excel at padel's volleys and overhead shots, while padel players develop soft hands and tactical awareness that translate well to doubles tennis. Many clubs now offer both sports, making it easy to switch between them depending on your mood, available partners, and physical state.

## The Bigger Picture

Tennis is not going anywhere. It remains one of the most watched and played sports globally, with a rich professional circuit, deep cultural roots, and billions of fans.

But padel is filling a space that tennis never fully occupied -- the space for a social, accessible, genuinely fun racket sport that works in cities, welcomes beginners, and creates community. The two sports can and do coexist. In many countries, they are growing together, with padel introducing new people to racket sports who then explore tennis as well.

The best sport is the one you will actually play. If you have never tried either, try both. If you are a tennis player curious about padel, book a court and bring three friends. You will not regret it.
