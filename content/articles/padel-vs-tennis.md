# Padel vs Tennis: Key Differences, Similarities, and Which is Right for You

The two sports share DNA. Identical scoring. A net dividing two opposing sides. Ground strokes, volleys, lobs, smashes. If you've played tennis, you'll feel oriented on a padel court within ten minutes.

You'll also notice the differences within ten seconds. Smaller court. Glass walls. Underhand serve. A racket with no strings.

Here's how the two games compare across every meaningful axis: court, racket, scoring, physicality, social dynamics, learning curve, cost, accessibility.

## The court

A standard tennis doubles court is **23.77 m × 10.97 m** (78 × 36 ft), open on all sides with run-off space beyond the lines.

A padel court is **20 m × 10 m** (66 × 33 ft), about a quarter the total area of a tennis court, enclosed by glass walls 3–4 m high. The walls are not just boundaries; they are part of the playing surface. The ball can rebound off them and stay in play.

That changes the practical economics. Two padel courts fit in the space of one tennis court, which makes padel viable in urban spots where land is expensive. Indoor padel facilities can run in converted warehouses, retail boxes, or purpose-built rooftop structures with much lower ceiling height than indoor tennis.

## The racket

A tennis racket is strung with synthetic gut or polyester across an oval frame, 68–73 cm long. Lightweight, with enormous power thanks to the trampoline effect of the strings.

A padel racket has no strings. The face is solid, perforated (drilled with holes for aerodynamics), made from carbon fiber, fiberglass, or a combination of both, with a foam core (EVA or FOAM). It's shorter than a tennis racket — typically 45–46 cm — and weighs 340–390 g.

The lack of strings is deliberate. Padel's smaller court would make a strung racket overpowered; balls would fly out of the enclosure on every hard shot. The solid face creates a game built around placement, spin, and touch rather than pure pace.

For beginners, a padel racket is easier to handle. Shorter length means less to coordinate. The face is generous relative to the overall size, so the sweet spot is forgiving, especially in round-shaped beginner models.

## Scoring

Identical. 15, 30, 40, deuce, advantage, game. Sets first to six games with a two-game lead, tiebreak at 6–6, matches best of three sets.

The one variation is padel's golden point rule, where deuce is decided by a single point rather than requiring a two-point margin. The receiving pair picks the side. Standard on the Premier Padel circuit. Tennis has no widespread equivalent, though some exhibition formats have experimented.

## The serve

In tennis, the serve is often the dominant shot in the game. Players toss the ball overhead and strike it with full force; speeds at the professional level routinely exceed 200 km/h. The serve wins points outright through aces. Building a powerful, reliable serve takes years.

In padel, the serve is underarm. The ball must be bounced before striking, and contact must be made at or below waist height. That single rule eliminates the serve as a weapon. Aces in padel are extremely rare, even at the professional level.

The implication for accessibility is large. Facing an experienced player's tennis serve can be uncomfortable or intimidating for a beginner. The padel underarm serve guarantees that every player, regardless of experience, can put the ball in play and participate in the rally from the first game.

## Physicality

Tennis is one of the most physically demanding sports in existence. Professional matches can last over five hours. Players cover ground laterally, forward, and backward across a large court. One- and two-handed groundstrokes need real upper-body strength. The overhead serve loads the shoulder, elbow, and wrist heavily over a career.

Padel is demanding in a different way. Smaller court, less total distance covered, but the game is built on short sprints, changes of direction, and explosive movements at the net. The underarm serve and lighter racket reduce shoulder load. Padel is generally easier on the body than tennis.

The wall play adds its own physical dimension. You read the ball off glass, adjust positioning to the rebound, and often play shots from unusual angles or while moving backward toward your own back wall. That trains spatial awareness and reflexes in ways tennis doesn't.

For older players, those recovering from injuries, or anyone looking for a less punishing racket sport, padel is the obvious answer. A lot of former tennis players have moved across precisely because they can keep competing at a high level with less wear.

That said, elite padel is no walk in the park. Pro players are seriously fit, the pace at the top is relentless, and the sport is as demanding as you choose to make it.

## Social dynamics

This is where the two diverge most sharply.

Tennis can be played as singles or doubles. Singles is the dominant format at both recreational and professional levels. Singles tennis is inherently individual: you against one opponent, no one to share the load with.

Padel is doubles, almost exclusively. Four players on every session. You need a partner, you need opponents, and the dynamic between four players defines the experience. Post-match drinks, dinner, debrief — that's part of padel culture in a way it usually isn't in casual tennis.

The social dimension is the most-cited reason people take up padel and keep playing. It's easier to convince three friends to come to a court than to find one evenly matched tennis opponent. And the doubles format means a skill gap between players matters less; a weaker partner alongside a stronger one can still compete and have a good time.

## Learning curve

Tennis has a notoriously steep curve. Just developing a reliable serve takes months. Groundstrokes require precise footwork, timing, and racket preparation. Most beginners spend their first sessions hitting into the net or over the fence and struggle to sustain a rally.

Padel's curve is gentler:

- The walls keep the ball in play. Shots that would be winners or errors in tennis bounce off the glass and return into the court, extending rallies and giving players more chances at the ball.
- The underarm serve is easy to learn. Most beginners can execute a legal serve within minutes.
- The shorter racket is more forgiving. The compact frame requires less precise technique to make solid contact.
- The smaller court means less ground to cover and a greater sense of being in control of the space.

Most padel beginners report enjoying competitive rallies in their first or second session. Reaching the same baseline of consistency in tennis takes a lot longer.

The ceiling, however, is high. Mastering wall play, building the full shot library (bandeja, víbora, bajada, chiquita), and learning the rhythms of doubles positioning and communication takes years. Easy to start, hard to master — close to the ideal combination for a sport seeking mass adoption.

## Cost

### Equipment

A beginner padel racket runs €40–€120. A solid intermediate racket is €120–€250. Pro-level rackets are €250–€450.

Beginner tennis rackets fall in a similar range, but the ongoing cost of stringing adds up. Tennis rackets need restringing every few weeks to a few months depending on how often you play, at €20–€50 per job. Padel rackets are solid; nothing to restring. They wear out eventually and need replacing, but the recurring cost is much lower.

Padel balls cost slightly less than tennis balls and are consumed at roughly the same rate.

### Court time

Court rental varies by location. Padel courts are generally comparable to or slightly more expensive than tennis courts per hour. But you split the cost four ways in padel (you always need four players), so the per-person cost is roughly half that of a tennis singles match.

### Clothing and shoes

Same athletic wear, same shoes (court shoes built for lateral movement). No meaningful cost difference.

### Overall

Factor in court-cost splitting, no stringing expense, and generally lower entry-level racket prices, and padel is typically cheaper to play regularly than tennis.

## Accessibility

Padel beats tennis on most accessibility measures:

- **Age range.** Padel is played competitively by kids as young as six and adults well into their seventies and eighties. Lower physical demands extend the playable age span.
- **Fitness level.** You don't need to be especially fit to enjoy a social game of padel. The smaller court and doubles format keep the physical floor low.
- **Prior experience.** Complete beginners can participate meaningfully from session one. Tennis typically needs more baseline skill before a real rally is possible.
- **Court availability.** Tennis courts are still more widely available globally, but padel courts are being built faster. In Spain, Italy, and several other European countries, padel courts already outnumber tennis courts.

Tennis keeps the edge in a few places. It's more widely taught in schools, has a deeper junior-development infrastructure in most countries, and offers solo practice (a wall, a ball machine) that padel doesn't easily replicate.

## Which one suits you

No wrong answer here. Both sports are excellent. Your preferences will point one way or the other.

**Choose padel if:**

- You want a social sport that guarantees interaction with other players
- You're new to racket sports and want a gentle learning curve
- You prefer doubles to singles
- You have joint concerns or want a less physically punishing option
- You enjoy strategic, tactical play over raw power
- You want to be part of a fast-growing community with fresh energy

**Choose tennis if:**

- You like solo competition and individual accountability
- You want to develop a powerful serve and aggressive baseline game
- You prefer a sport with a longer history and deeper competitive infrastructure
- You enjoy the physical challenge of covering a large court
- You want the option to practise alone
- You're pursuing a junior or collegiate athletic path where tennis infrastructure is more established

### Or play both

A growing number of players play both. The skills are complementary. Tennis players often excel at padel's volleys and overheads. Padel players develop soft hands and tactical awareness that translate well to doubles tennis. Many clubs now offer both, making it easy to switch depending on mood, available partners, and physical state.

The best sport is the one you'll actually play. If you've never tried either, try both. If you're a tennis player curious about padel, book a court and bring three friends.
