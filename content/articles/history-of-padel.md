# The History of Padel: From Mexico to Global Phenomenon

Padel is often described as the world's fastest-growing sport, but its origins trace back to a single backyard in Acapulco, Mexico, more than half a century ago. What started as one man's improvisation on his property has grown into a global phenomenon played by over 30 million people across more than 110 countries.

This is the story of how padel went from a quirky Mexican invention to a professional sport with its own international tour, broadcast deals, and a serious bid for Olympic inclusion.

## 1969: The Birth of Padel in Acapulco

The story begins with **Enrique Corcuera**, a wealthy Mexican businessman who wanted to build a tennis-style court at his vacation home in Acapulco. The problem was simple: he did not have enough space. His property was bordered by walls on two sides, leaving insufficient room for a full tennis court.

Rather than abandon the idea, Corcuera adapted. He built a smaller court -- roughly 20 meters by 10 meters -- and incorporated the existing walls into the playing area. Instead of treating the walls as out-of-bounds, he made them part of the game. Players could hit the ball off the walls, keeping rallies alive and adding a new strategic dimension.

Corcuera called his creation "padel" (sometimes spelled "paddle" in early references), drew up a basic set of rules, and began playing with friends and family. The enclosed court, the underhand serve, the doubles-only format, and the use of walls as active playing surfaces were all present from the start.

What Corcuera likely did not realize was that he had invented a sport that would eventually rival tennis in popularity across multiple continents.

## The 1970s: From Mexico to Marbella

The critical turning point in padel's history came when **Alfonso de Hohenlohe**, a Spanish aristocrat and socialite who owned the famous Marbella Club on Spain's Costa del Sol, visited Corcuera in Acapulco and played the game.

Hohenlohe was captivated. He saw the potential for padel as a social sport -- accessible, fun, and perfectly suited to the leisure culture of southern Spain. In the mid-1970s, he built padel courts at the Marbella Club, introducing the sport to Spanish high society and the international jet set that frequented the resort.

The timing was fortuitous. Marbella in the 1970s was a magnet for wealthy Europeans, Latin Americans, and celebrities. Padel became a fashionable pastime among this crowd, and the sport began to spread through social networks of the affluent.

### Argentina Catches Fire

Around the same time, padel crossed the Atlantic in the other direction. Argentine visitors to Spain discovered the sport and brought it home, where it found remarkably fertile ground.

Argentina's strong sporting culture, love of social gathering, and existing infrastructure of club sports made it the perfect environment for padel to thrive. By the late 1970s and into the 1980s, padel courts were springing up across Buenos Aires and beyond.

Argentina's adoption of padel was explosive. The sport spread from exclusive clubs to neighborhood facilities, from the capital to the provinces. Within a decade, Argentina had more padel courts per capita than any country in the world, a distinction it held for decades.

## The 1980s and 1990s: Spain and Argentina Lead

### Spain: The Second Home

In Spain, padel grew steadily through the 1980s. What had started as an amusement for Marbella's elite gradually spread to mainstream sports clubs across Andalusia, then Madrid, Barcelona, and the rest of the country.

Several factors drove padel's growth in Spain:

- **Social appeal:** Padel is inherently social. Four players on a small court means constant interaction, and the lower skill floor compared to tennis means beginners can enjoy rallies from day one.
- **Club culture:** Spain's extensive network of sports clubs, community centers, and municipal facilities provided ready-made venues for padel courts.
- **Real estate efficiency:** A padel court takes up significantly less space than a tennis court, making it easier and cheaper to build, especially in urban areas where space is at a premium.
- **Climate:** Spain's warm, dry climate made outdoor padel a year-round activity in much of the country.

By the early 1990s, Spain had thousands of padel courts, and the sport had become a mainstream recreational activity rather than a niche pursuit.

### Argentina: The Padel Capital

Argentina's love affair with padel only deepened through the 1980s and 1990s. The country developed a professional competitive scene earlier than Spain, and Argentine players dominated the sport for decades.

The cultural fit was remarkable. In Argentina, padel became as much a social ritual as a sport. Groups of friends would book a court, play for an hour, and then spend another hour sharing mate or grilling at the club. Padel was woven into the fabric of Argentine social life in a way few sports achieve.

By the mid-1990s, estimates placed the number of padel courts in Argentina at over 40,000, with millions of regular players. The country produced generation after generation of world-class players who would later dominate the professional circuit.

## The 2000s: Professional Circuits Emerge

### The World Padel Tour

The formalization of professional padel began in earnest in the 2000s. Spain launched the **Padel Pro Tour** in 2005, which evolved into the **World Padel Tour (WPT)** in 2013. The WPT became the dominant professional circuit, organizing tournaments primarily in Spain but with increasing international stops.

The WPT professionalized the sport significantly:

- Standardized tournament formats and rankings
- Introduced prize money and sponsorship deals
- Produced broadcast content that brought padel to television audiences
- Established a clear pathway for aspiring professional players

Spanish and Argentine players dominated the WPT, with legendary figures like Fernando Belasteguin (widely considered the greatest padel player of all time), Juan Martin Diaz, Alejandra Salazar, and Marta Marrero establishing themselves as icons of the sport.

### Fernando Belasteguin: The GOAT

No history of padel is complete without acknowledging Fernando "Bela" Belasteguin. The Argentine left-hander held the world number one ranking for an unprecedented 16 consecutive years (2002-2018), a record of dominance unmatched in virtually any professional sport. Belasteguin's consistency, tactical intelligence, and longevity made him the most recognizable face in padel and an ambassador for the sport worldwide.

## The 2010s: European Expansion

While Spain and Argentina remained the heartlands of padel, the 2010s saw the sport expand rapidly across Europe:

### Italy
Italy became one of padel's biggest growth stories. Starting from a relatively small base, the country experienced a padel boom in the mid-2010s that accelerated dramatically. By 2022, Italy had become the fastest-growing padel market in Europe, with thousands of new courts opening annually.

### Sweden and Scandinavia
Sweden emerged as a surprising padel powerhouse. The country embraced the sport enthusiastically, building indoor courts to accommodate the Nordic climate. Sweden now has one of the highest concentrations of padel courts per capita in Europe.

### Portugal
Portugal's proximity to Spain and similar climate made it a natural fit for padel. The country saw steady growth through the 2010s, with both recreational and competitive scenes developing.

### France, Belgium, and the Netherlands
Northern European countries began building indoor padel facilities, recognizing the sport's appeal even in less favorable climates. France, in particular, experienced significant growth, aided by the French Tennis Federation's decision to embrace padel under its umbrella.

### The Middle East
The UAE, Qatar, Saudi Arabia, and other Gulf states invested heavily in padel infrastructure, attracted by the sport's social appeal and compatibility with the region's climate (when played in air-conditioned indoor facilities or during cooler months).

## 2022: The Premier Padel Revolution

The most transformative moment in modern padel history came in 2022 with the launch of **Premier Padel**, the official international tour sanctioned by the **International Padel Federation (FIP)** in partnership with the **Professional Padel Association (PPA)** and **Qatar Sports Investments (QSI)**.

Premier Padel represented a seismic shift:

- It broke the World Padel Tour's monopoly on professional padel
- The FIP, as the sport's international governing body, backed Premier Padel as the official tour
- QSI (the same investment group behind Paris Saint-Germain football club) provided significant financial backing
- The tour attracted top players with higher prize money and a global tournament calendar

The early years of Premier Padel were marked by political tensions with the WPT, legal disputes over player contracts, and competition for tournament hosting rights. But by 2024, the landscape had largely consolidated, with Premier Padel establishing itself as the premier professional circuit.

### Qatar Airways Sponsorship

In a sign of padel's growing commercial appeal, Qatar Airways became the title sponsor of the Premier Padel tour, lending the event global brand recognition and associating the sport with one of the world's most prominent airlines.

## 2024-2026: The Golden Age

The current era represents what many are calling padel's golden age:

- **Over 30 million players** worldwide across more than 110 countries
- **70,000+ padel courts** globally, with thousands more under construction
- **Premier Padel** broadcasts reaching audiences in over 190 territories
- **beIN Sports** securing broadcast rights across 39 territories for the 2026 season
- The sport's inclusion in the **2026 Asian Games** in Aichi-Nagoya, Japan
- A credible bid for inclusion in the **2032 Brisbane Olympics**
- Major investment from sports groups, private equity, and sovereign wealth funds

The player landscape has also evolved. The dominance of Arturo Coello and Agustin Tapia on the men's side, and Gemma Triay and Delfina Brea on the women's side, has produced a new generation of stars who are pushing the sport's profile to unprecedented heights.

## Key Milestones Timeline

- **1969:** Enrique Corcuera builds the first padel court in Acapulco, Mexico
- **Mid-1970s:** Alfonso de Hohenlohe introduces padel to Marbella, Spain
- **Late 1970s:** Padel spreads to Argentina
- **1991:** The International Padel Federation (FIP) is founded
- **2005:** The Padel Pro Tour launches in Spain
- **2013:** The World Padel Tour (WPT) is established
- **2022:** Premier Padel launches as the FIP-backed global tour
- **2024:** FIP gains ARISF status with the International Olympic Committee
- **2025:** Padel recognized by the Olympic Council of Asia; included in 2026 Asian Games
- **2026:** Premier Padel continues global expansion with new broadcast deals and tournament venues

## The Road Ahead

Padel's trajectory shows no signs of slowing. The sport continues to grow in markets as diverse as the United States, Japan, Australia, and across Africa. The combination of accessibility (easy to learn, social, less physically demanding than tennis), entertainment value (long rallies, spectacular wall plays), and strong institutional backing (FIP, Premier Padel, Qatar Sports Investments) positions padel for continued global expansion.

The question is no longer whether padel will become a major global sport. It already is. The question is how far it can go, and whether the Brisbane 2032 Olympics might mark padel's arrival on the biggest stage of all.
