# The History of Padel: From Mexico to Global Phenomenon

The whole sport started because one man's house was the wrong shape. Enrique Corcuera, a wealthy Mexican businessman, wanted a tennis court at his Acapulco home in 1969 but didn't have enough room for one. So he built something smaller, walled in the gaps with what was already on the property, and decided the walls were in play. That was padel.

Fifty-seven years later, his improvisation is the world's fastest-growing sport. Played by 35 million people in more than 150 countries. Backed by Qatar Sports Investments. Bidding for an Olympic slot. Here is how it got from a Mexican backyard to here.

## 1969: Acapulco

The story begins with **Enrique Corcuera**. He wanted a tennis-style court at his vacation home and didn't have the footprint for it. His property was bordered by walls on two sides, leaving insufficient room for a full tennis court.

Rather than abandon the idea, he adapted. He built a smaller court (roughly 20 m × 10 m) and incorporated the existing walls into the playing area. Instead of treating the walls as out-of-bounds, he made them part of the game. The ball could be played off the walls, keeping rallies alive and adding a strategic dimension that no other racket sport offered.

Corcuera called his creation "padel" (sometimes spelled "paddle" in early references), drew up a basic set of rules, and began playing with friends and family. The enclosed court, the underhand serve, the doubles-only format, and the use of walls as active playing surfaces were all present from day one.

He almost certainly did not realise he had invented a sport that would eventually rival tennis across multiple continents.

## The 1970s: from Mexico to Marbella

The turning point came when **Alfonso de Hohenlohe**, a Spanish aristocrat who owned the Marbella Club on the Costa del Sol, visited Corcuera in Acapulco and played the game.

Hohenlohe was captivated. He saw the potential for padel as a social sport: accessible, fun, perfectly suited to the leisure culture of southern Spain. In the mid-1970s, he built padel courts at the Marbella Club, introducing the sport to Spanish high society and the international jet set that frequented the resort.

The timing was lucky. Marbella in the 1970s was a magnet for wealthy Europeans, Latin Americans, and celebrities. Padel became a fashionable pastime among that crowd, and the sport began to spread through the social networks of the affluent.

### Argentina catches fire

Padel crossed the Atlantic in the other direction at almost the same time. Argentine visitors to Spain discovered the game and brought it home, where it found unusually fertile ground.

Argentina's strong sporting culture, love of social gathering, and existing infrastructure of club sports made it the perfect environment for padel to thrive. By the late 1970s and into the 1980s, padel courts were springing up across Buenos Aires and beyond.

Argentina's adoption was explosive. The sport spread from exclusive clubs to neighbourhood facilities, from the capital to the provinces. Within a decade, Argentina had more padel courts per capita than any country in the world, a distinction it held for decades.

## The 1980s and 1990s: Spain and Argentina lead

### Spain: the second home

In Spain, padel grew steadily through the 1980s. What had started as an amusement for Marbella's elite gradually spread to mainstream sports clubs across Andalucía, then Madrid, Barcelona, and the rest of the country.

What drove the growth:

- **Social appeal.** Four players on a small court means constant interaction. The lower skill floor compared to tennis means beginners can enjoy rallies from the first session.
- **Club culture.** Spain's network of sports clubs, community centres, and municipal facilities provided ready-made venues.
- **Real-estate efficiency.** A padel court takes significantly less space than a tennis court, easier and cheaper to build, especially in urban areas.
- **Climate.** Spain's warm, dry climate made outdoor padel a year-round activity in much of the country.

By the early 1990s, Spain had thousands of padel courts and the sport had become a mainstream recreational activity rather than a niche pursuit.

### Argentina: the padel capital

Argentina's relationship with padel only deepened through the 1980s and 1990s. The country developed a professional competitive scene earlier than Spain, and Argentine players dominated the sport for decades.

The cultural fit was unusually deep. In Argentina, padel became as much a social ritual as a sport. Groups of friends would book a court, play for an hour, and then spend another hour over mate or grilling at the club. Padel was woven into the fabric of Argentine social life in a way few sports achieve.

By the mid-1990s, estimates placed the number of padel courts in Argentina at over 40,000, with millions of regular players. The country produced generation after generation of world-class players who would later dominate the professional circuit.

## The 2000s: professional circuits emerge

### The World Padel Tour

The formalisation of professional padel began in earnest in the 2000s. Spain launched the **Padel Pro Tour** in 2005, which evolved into the **World Padel Tour (WPT)** in 2013. The WPT became the dominant professional circuit, organising tournaments primarily in Spain but with increasing international stops.

The WPT professionalised the sport meaningfully:

- Standardised tournament formats and rankings
- Introduced prize money and sponsorship deals
- Produced broadcast content that brought padel to television audiences
- Established a clear pathway for aspiring professional players

Spanish and Argentine players dominated the WPT, with legendary figures like Fernando Belasteguín (widely considered the greatest padel player of all time), Juan Martín Díaz, Alejandra Salazar, and Marta Marrero establishing themselves as icons of the sport.

### Fernando Belasteguín: the GOAT

No history of padel is complete without Fernando "Bela" Belasteguín. The Argentine left-hander held the world number-one ranking for an unprecedented **16 consecutive years (2002–2018)**, a streak of dominance unmatched in any professional sport. Belasteguín's consistency, tactical intelligence, and longevity made him the most recognisable face in padel and an ambassador for the sport worldwide.

## The 2010s: European expansion

While Spain and Argentina remained the heartlands, the 2010s saw the sport expand rapidly across Europe.

### Italy

Italy became one of padel's biggest growth stories. Starting from a small base, the country experienced a padel boom in the mid-2010s that accelerated dramatically. By 2022, Italy was the fastest-growing padel market in Europe, with thousands of new courts opening annually.

### Sweden and Scandinavia

Sweden emerged as an unexpected padel powerhouse. The country embraced the sport enthusiastically, building indoor courts to accommodate the Nordic climate. Sweden now has one of the highest concentrations of padel courts per capita in Europe.

### Portugal

Portugal's proximity to Spain and similar climate made it a natural fit. The country saw steady growth through the 2010s, with both recreational and competitive scenes developing.

### France, Belgium, the Netherlands

Northern European countries began building indoor padel facilities, recognising the sport's appeal even in less favourable climates. France, in particular, experienced significant growth, helped by the French Tennis Federation's decision to bring padel under its umbrella.

### The Middle East

The UAE, Qatar, Saudi Arabia, and other Gulf states invested heavily in padel infrastructure, attracted by the sport's social appeal and its compatibility with the region's climate (in air-conditioned indoor facilities or during cooler months).

## 2022: the Premier Padel revolution

The most consequential moment in modern padel history came in 2022 with the launch of **Premier Padel**, the official international tour sanctioned by the **International Padel Federation (FIP)** in partnership with the **Professional Padel Association (PPA)** and **Qatar Sports Investments (QSI)**.

Premier Padel represented a seismic shift:

- It broke the World Padel Tour's monopoly on professional padel
- The FIP, as the sport's international governing body, backed Premier Padel as the official tour
- QSI (the same investment group behind Paris Saint-Germain football club) provided significant financial backing
- The tour attracted top players with higher prize money and a global tournament calendar

The early years of Premier Padel were marked by political tensions with the WPT, legal disputes over player contracts, and competition for tournament hosting rights. By 2024, the landscape had largely consolidated, with Premier Padel established as the premier professional circuit.

### Qatar Airways sponsorship

In a sign of padel's growing commercial appeal, Qatar Airways became the title sponsor of the Premier Padel tour, lending the event global brand recognition and associating the sport with one of the world's most prominent airlines.

## 2024–2026: the current era

What's happening now:

- **35 million players** worldwide across more than 150 countries
- **70,000+ padel courts** globally, with thousands more under construction
- **Premier Padel** broadcasts reaching audiences in over 190 territories
- **beIN Sports** holding broadcast rights across 39 territories for the 2026 season
- The sport included in the **2026 Asian Games** in Aichi-Nagoya, Japan
- A credible bid for inclusion in the **2032 Brisbane Olympics**
- Major investment from sports groups, private equity, and sovereign wealth funds

The player landscape has evolved. The dominance of Arturo Coello and Agustín Tapia on the men's side, and Gemma Triay and Delfina Brea on the women's side, has produced a new generation of stars who are pushing the sport's profile to unprecedented heights.

## Key milestones

- **1969** — Enrique Corcuera builds the first padel court in Acapulco, Mexico
- **Mid-1970s** — Alfonso de Hohenlohe introduces padel to Marbella, Spain
- **Late 1970s** — Padel spreads to Argentina
- **1991** — The International Padel Federation (FIP) is founded
- **2005** — The Padel Pro Tour launches in Spain
- **2013** — The World Padel Tour (WPT) is established
- **2022** — Premier Padel launches as the FIP-backed global tour
- **2024** — FIP gains ARISF status with the International Olympic Committee
- **2025** — Padel recognised by the Olympic Council of Asia; included in 2026 Asian Games
- **2026** — Premier Padel continues global expansion with new broadcast deals and tournament venues

## What's next

The question is no longer whether padel will become a major global sport. It already is. The question is how far it can go, and whether Brisbane 2032 might mark padel's arrival on the biggest stage of all.
