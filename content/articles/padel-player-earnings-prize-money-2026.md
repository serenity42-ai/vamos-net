# How Much Do Professional Padel Players Earn? Prize Money, Sponsorships, and the 2026 Reality

The two best padel players in the world, Arturo Coello and Agustín Tapia, earned **€486,645 each** in prize money in 2025. Once you add sponsorship contracts (reportedly up to €1 million per year for the top pair), each is making well past €1 million annually.

That's the top of the pyramid. The middle is harder. The bottom doesn't break even.

This is the complete financial picture of professional padel in 2026: prize pools, sponsorship structures, top-end income, and the steep cliff that begins below the top 20.

## Premier Padel 2026: the prize money structure

The 2026 Qatar Airways Premier Padel Tour operates a three-tier tournament structure, with prize money scaling accordingly.

### Major tournaments

Majors are padel's Grand Slams. The 2026 calendar includes Majors in Doha, Rome, and Paris, among other locations.

- **Total prize pool per category** (men's or women's): €500,000
- **Winners (per player):** ~€52,500
- **Finalists:** ~€27,500
- **Semi-finalists:** ~€15,000
- **Quarter-finalists:** ~€9,000
- **Round of 16:** ~€5,000–€6,000
- **Round of 32:** ~€2,500–€3,500

Padel is doubles, so the winning pair collectively takes home approximately €105,000 at a Major, split equally between the two players (Padel Earnings, 2026).

### P1 tournaments

P1 events are the middle tier. The 2026 Riyadh Season P1 had a total prize pool of €260,000 per category.

- **Winners (per player):** ~€25,500
- **Finalists:** ~€14,000–€15,000
- **Semi-finalists:** ~€8,000–€9,000
- **Quarter-finalists:** ~€4,500–€5,500
- **Round of 16:** ~€2,500–€3,000

In 2025, P1 tournaments offered total prize pools around €470,000 (both categories combined), with winners earning approximately €35,000 per player (Padel Magazine, February 2025; Padel Earnings, 2025). The 2026 structure represents a slight adjustment in distribution while maintaining comparable total purses.

### P2 tournaments

P2 events are the most accessible tier of the Premier Padel circuit, with smaller draws and lower prize pools.

- **Total prize pool per category:** ~€147,500
- **Winners (per player):** ~€15,000
- **Finalists:** ~€8,000–€10,000
- **Semi-finalists:** ~€4,500–€5,500
- **Earlier rounds:** €1,000–€3,000

### Tour Finals

The season-ending Tour Finals in Barcelona (2025 edition) feature the top-ranked pairs competing for a substantial purse. In the 2025 Barcelona Finals, winners Coello and Tapia each earned €52,500. Finalists Federico Chingotto and Alejandro Galán each took €30,000. On the women's side, finalists Claudia Fernández and Bea González each earned €26,250, with the runners-up Gemma Triay receiving the same amount (Padel Earnings, December 2025).

### FIP Tour events

Below the Premier Padel circuit, the CUPRA FIP Tour includes Platinum, Gold, Silver, and Bronze events with significantly smaller purses. A FIP Silver event might offer total prize money of just a few thousand euros, with winners receiving as little as €720 (Padel Earnings, 2026). These events are critical for ranking points and development but offer negligible income.

## What the top players actually earn

### Prize money leaders

The 2025 season provided clear data on top-end earnings.

**Men's:**

- **Arturo Coello and Agustín Tapia:** €486,645 each in prize money for the 2025 season (Padel Tonic, January 2026). In 2024, they earned €492,375 each after winning 14 titles in 21 tournaments. Their 2024 match record was 94 wins from 101 matches, a dominance ratio rarely seen in any professional sport.
- **Alejandro Galán and Federico Chingotto:** €380,531 each in 2025 season prize money (Padel Tonic, January 2026).
- **Juan Lebrón:** €194,531 in 2024, having won 59 of 79 matches paired with Franco Stupaczuk (Insider Media, May 2025).
- **Franco Stupaczuk:** €198,094 in 2024, winning 65 of 87 matches.

**Women's:**

- **Gemma Triay and Delfi Brea:** among the top earners on the women's circuit. Triay earned €26,250 at the 2025 Barcelona Finals as a finalist.
- **Bea González and Claudia Fernández:** dominated the 2025 Finals and were consistently among the highest-earning women's pairs throughout the season.

For context: the top men's pair earned just under €500,000 each from prize money alone in 2025. A significant increase from the pre-unification era, when top players might earn €100,000–€200,000 annually in prize money.

### Sponsorship and endorsement income

Prize money tells only part of the story. For the top 10–15 players, **sponsorships represent 70–80% of total income** (Padel60, June 2025).

Leading brands in padel player sponsorship include:

- **Bullpadel.** One of the most active sponsors, signing exclusive racket and apparel deals with multiple top players.
- **Head.** Major racket sponsor with a growing stable of padel players.
- **Wilson.** Entered padel sponsorship aggressively, also serves as an official Premier Padel partner.
- **Adidas / All For Padel.** Equipment and apparel.
- **Nox.** Popular racket brand with a strong player roster.
- **Red Bull.** Sponsors select athletes with broader media and lifestyle profiles.
- **CUPRA.** The automotive brand sponsors the FIP Tour and individual players.

For elite players like Coello and Tapia, sponsorship contracts reportedly reach up to €1 million per year (Padel60, June 2025). Combined with prize money, the top male pair can earn €1.5 million or more annually each.

Other income sources for top players:

- **Exhibition matches and clinics:** €5,000–€20,000 per appearance
- **Social media:** players with large followings (100,000+ on Instagram) can command €1,000–€5,000+ per sponsored post
- **Academy affiliations:** some players are associated with padel academies, earning fees for lending their name and occasional presence
- **Equipment royalties:** top players may receive royalties on signature racket models

## The financial reality below the top 20

The prize money structure creates a steep cliff below the top echelon. A pair that consistently reaches the quarter-finals of P1 events and the Round of 16 at Majors might earn €40,000–€70,000 each annually in prize money. After deducting travel costs (flights, hotels, coaching, physiotherapy, equipment), which can run €30,000–€60,000 per year on the professional circuit, the net income from playing is modest to negative.

Players ranked outside the top 50 face a stark reality: prize money from P2 events and FIP Tour stops often does not cover the cost of competing. A first-round exit at a P2 event pays approximately €1,000–€2,000 per player. A player competing in 15–20 events per year and losing in early rounds might earn €15,000–€25,000 in gross prize money. Far below the cost of maintaining a professional career.

As one Reddit user observed, echoing a common sentiment in the padel community: "It looks hard for the players below rank 60 to have a nice life just by playing padel" (Reddit, r/padel, 2023). Prize money has increased since that observation, but the fundamental economics remain challenging for the middle and lower ranks.

Most professional padel players outside the top 20 supplement their income through coaching, academy work, or separate employment. The sport hasn't yet reached the economic maturity to support a fully professional field of 100+ players, the way tennis does.

## Padel vs tennis: the prize money gap

The comparison to tennis illustrates both how far padel has come and how far it has to go.

**Grand Slam tennis (2025):**

- Wimbledon singles champion: approximately £2.7 million (~€3.1 million)
- Roland Garros singles champion: €2.4 million
- First-round loser at a Grand Slam: ~€50,000–€80,000

**Premier Padel Major (2026):**

- Major winners (per player): ~€52,500
- First-round loser at a Major: ~€2,500–€3,500

The gap is staggering. A Grand Slam tennis champion earns roughly 50× more than a Premier Padel Major champion. Even a first-round Grand Slam loser in tennis earns more than a padel Major winner.

At the mid-tier, a tennis player ranked 50th in the world typically earns $1–$2 million annually from prize money alone. A padel player of equivalent ranking earns perhaps €30,000–€50,000 in prize money.

The trajectory matters. When the ATP Tour was at a comparable stage of its development (late 1970s / early 1980s), prize money was a fraction of what it is today. Padel's prize money has roughly tripled since 2021, and the trend line is upward. The sport's broadcast deals, sponsor portfolio, and event infrastructure are all expanding rapidly.

**ATP/WTA total annual prize money:** approximately $800 million+ across all sanctioned events.
**Premier Padel estimated total annual prize money:** approximately €10–€15 million across all events (a rough estimate based on available per-tournament data).

The ratio is approximately 50:1, which roughly mirrors the court revenue comparison between the sports.

## Equal prize money: padel's progressive stance

One area where padel compares favourably to many sports is gender pay equity. The World Padel Tour announced equal prize money for men and women on March 8, 2022 — International Women's Day. Premier Padel has maintained this commitment since the unification of the tours.

At every level of Premier Padel — Majors, P1, and P2 events — the prize pools for men's and women's draws are identical. The €500,000 Major prize pool applies equally to men and women. P1 and P2 purses are split evenly.

This is notable because:

- In tennis, equal prize money at Grand Slams was achieved only after decades of advocacy (Wimbledon didn't offer equal prizes until 2007)
- Many professional sports still have significant gender pay gaps
- Padel implemented equal prize money relatively early in its professionalisation

The practical effect is that the top women's players earn prize money comparable to their male counterparts at the same ranking level. Where the gap opens is in sponsorship: male players still tend to command higher sponsorship fees, reflecting broader media exposure and viewership patterns. The 40% female participation rate in padel provides a strong base for women's player marketability.

## Sponsorship structure in detail

The commercial architecture around padel player sponsorship operates at several levels.

### Tour-level sponsors

Qatar Airways (title sponsor), Red Bull (broadcast partner and athlete sponsor), Wilson, Bullpadel, Playtomic, MejorSet, Betsson, NTT Data, and Mondo all have tour-level deals with Premier Padel (Sportcal, May 2025). CUPRA sponsors the FIP Tour. These brands gain court-side visibility, digital content integration, and association with the sport's premium events.

### Equipment sponsors

Every professional player has a racket deal, though terms vary dramatically. Top-5 players receive significant annual retainers (reportedly €200,000–€500,000+ for the very best) plus performance bonuses, equipment, and sometimes royalties on signature racket models. Players ranked 10–30 may receive €20,000–€100,000 in equipment sponsorship. Players outside the top 50 may receive free equipment with minimal or no financial compensation.

### Apparel and footwear

Brands including Adidas, Joma, Asics, Bullpadel, and NOX provide apparel and footwear deals. Typically less lucrative than racket sponsorships for most players but can be significant for top players who serve as brand ambassadors.

### Lifestyle and non-endemic brands

As padel's cultural profile grows, non-endemic brands are entering the sponsorship space. Automotive (CUPRA), airlines (Qatar Airways), beverage (Red Bull), and betting (Betsson) see padel's demographic — young, affluent, social, urban — as an attractive audience. Individual player deals with lifestyle brands are growing but remain the preserve of the top 5–10 most marketable players.

## Trends: is player compensation growing?

Yes, and significantly.

1. **Prize money escalation.** Total Premier Padel prize money has roughly tripled since the tour's launch in 2022. Major prize pools have grown from approximately €300,000 to €500,000 per category.
2. **New tournament stops.** The expansion of the tour calendar to 21+ events in 2026, including high-profile stops in the US and Middle East, creates more earning opportunities.
3. **Broadcast revenue growth.** beIN Media Group's renewed deal for 39 territories, Red Bull TV's global streaming coverage, and expanding regional broadcast partnerships inject revenue that can be redistributed to prize pools.
4. **Tour-level sponsorship growth.** The addition of Qatar Airways as title sponsor in 2024 and the growing roster of commercial partners indicate rising commercial revenue.
5. **Star Point system.** The new ranking system introduced for 2026 incentivises participation across the full calendar, which should increase total player earning opportunities.

Several structural factors constrain the pace of growth:

- **Doubles format.** Prize money is split between two players, halving per-capita earnings compared to tennis singles.
- **Early-stage media rights.** Padel's broadcast rights don't yet command the per-event fees that would support ATP/WTA-level purses.
- **Market size.** While growing rapidly, padel's total commercial structure is a fraction of tennis.
- **Travel costs.** As the tour becomes more global (Miami, Riyadh, Asia), travel expenses increase, potentially offsetting prize money gains for lower-ranked players.

## What this means for the sport's future

Professional padel is at an inflection point. The top players are earning well — comfortably into seven figures when sponsorships are included — and the prize money trajectory is strongly upward. The sport implemented equal pay from the outset, avoiding decades of controversy that plagued tennis.

But the professional pyramid remains narrow. Only 30–40 players on each side (men's and women's) can realistically sustain a full-time professional career from padel income alone. Below that threshold, players must supplement with coaching, academies, or other work.

For the sport to reach its full potential, several things need to happen:

- **Media rights must mature.** Meaningful per-event broadcast fees would let prize pools scale dramatically.
- **The US and Asian markets must develop.** Larger audiences in high-value advertising markets would attract bigger sponsors.
- **The tournament calendar must keep expanding.** More events at all tiers create more earning opportunities across the ranking spectrum.
- **Player unions must strengthen.** The Professional Padel Association (PPA) needs to effectively advocate for player financial interests as the tour's commercial value grows.

The comparison to early-career tennis is instructive. When the ATP was founded in 1972, total prize money on the men's tour was approximately $5 million. Today it exceeds $400 million. Padel doesn't need to replicate that scale to be successful, but the trajectory from $10–15 million in total prize money to $50–100 million is achievable within a decade if the sport's commercial momentum continues.

For players considering a professional padel career in 2026, the financial calculus is improving but remains demanding. The top of the pyramid offers genuine wealth. The middle offers a viable career with supplementary income. The base requires passion, resilience, and usually a backup plan.

The money in professional padel is growing. The question is whether it's growing fast enough to match the ambitions of a sport that increasingly sees itself as the world's fastest-growing competitor to tennis.
