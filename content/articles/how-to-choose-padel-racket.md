# How to Choose a Padel Racket: Shape, Weight, and Balance Guide

Five variables determine how a padel racket plays: **shape, weight, balance, core, and face material**. Get the right combination for your level and style, and the racket disappears under your hand. Get it wrong, and you'll fight every shot.

Tennis players come to padel and assume one racket is much like another. They aren't. A diamond-shaped racket in beginner hands plays nothing like a round racket in the same hands. Here's what each variable does, and how to combine them.

## Padel racket basics

What's different about padel rackets:

- **No strings.** A solid hitting surface with drilled holes for aerodynamics.
- **Maximum dimensions.** 45.5 cm long, 26 cm wide, 38 mm thick (FIP regulations).
- **Weight range.** Typically 340–390 g.
- **Core material.** Foam or rubber compound that determines feel and power.
- **Face material.** Carbon fiber, fiberglass, or composite that affects spin, power, and durability.
- **Wrist strap.** Required by the rules. Every padel racket must have one, and players must wear it during play.

## The three shapes

Shape is the single most important factor in how a padel racket plays. The three primary shapes each give you a fundamentally different experience.

### Round

**Best for:** beginners, intermediate players, defensive players, control-oriented players.

The round racket has the widest hitting surface and the largest sweet spot of any shape. Its balance point sits lower (closer to the handle), making it easier to manoeuvre and more forgiving on off-centre hits.

Characteristics:

- Largest sweet spot
- Low balance point (head-light to even)
- Maximum control and touch
- Easier to manoeuvre at the net
- Less raw power than other shapes
- Most forgiving on mishits

**Why choose round:** if you're learning the game, this is your first racket. The large sweet spot reduces errors; the low balance makes the racket feel lighter and more controllable. Even at the professional level, plenty of players choose round rackets for their superior control and defensive capabilities.

**Popular round rackets:** Bullpadel Vertex, Head Alpha, Babolat Viper Air.

### Diamond

**Best for:** advanced players, power hitters, aggressive baseliners.

The diamond racket has its widest point near the top of the head, creating a high balance point and concentrating mass at the tip. Maximum power on overhead shots and smashes, at the cost of control and manoeuvrability.

Characteristics:

- Smallest sweet spot
- High balance point (head-heavy)
- Maximum power, especially on overheads
- More difficult to manoeuvre at the net
- Less forgiving on off-centre hits
- Requires strong technique and wrist strength

**Why choose diamond:** for players who have mastered their technique and want to maximise attacking power. If you play an aggressive style with lots of smashes and overheads, a diamond racket amplifies that game. The small sweet spot punishes mishits more severely than any other shape.

**Popular diamond rackets:** Adidas Adipower, Bullpadel Hack, Nox AT10 Genius.

### Teardrop

**Best for:** intermediate to advanced players, all-around players, those wanting a balance of power and control.

The teardrop sits between round and diamond. Medium sweet spot, moderate balance point. The most versatile shape and the most popular choice among competitive recreational players.

Characteristics:

- Medium sweet spot
- Medium balance point
- Good balance of power and control
- Reasonable manoeuvrability
- Versatile for different playing styles
- Moderate forgiveness on mishits

**Why choose teardrop:** if you're not sure what shape to choose, or if you play a varied game that mixes defence and attack, the teardrop is the safest bet. It doesn't excel in any single area as much as the round (control) or the diamond (power), but it performs well across all situations.

**Popular teardrop rackets:** Babolat Viper, Wilson Bela Pro, Head Delta Pro.

## Weight: finding your range

Padel rackets typically weigh 340–390 g. The weight you choose affects power, control, manoeuvrability, and the strain on your arm.

### Light (340–355 g)

- Easier to swing quickly
- Better manoeuvrability at the net
- Less power on overhead shots
- Lower risk of arm fatigue and injury
- **Best for:** beginners, players with arm issues, players who prioritise speed and touch.

### Medium (355–370 g)

- Good balance of power and control
- Manageable for most players
- Versatile for different shot types
- **Best for:** most intermediate and recreational players.

### Heavy (370–390 g)

- Maximum power on all shots
- More stable on contact (less racket twist)
- Slower to manoeuvre
- Higher risk of fatigue and overuse injuries
- **Best for:** advanced players with strong technique and good physical conditioning.

### A note on overweighting

Some advanced players add lead tape or other weights to customise their racket's weight and balance. Common at the professional level. Should only be attempted by experienced players who understand how weight distribution affects racket performance.

## Balance: head-light, even, head-heavy

Balance describes where the centre of mass sits along the racket's length. Measured in millimetres from the base of the handle.

### Head-light (low balance)

- Balance point below 260 mm
- Feels lighter in the hand
- Greater manoeuvrability
- More control, less power
- Typical of round rackets

### Even (medium)

- Balance point around 260–270 mm
- Balanced feel between power and control
- Versatile performance
- Typical of teardrop rackets

### Head-heavy (high balance)

- Balance point above 270 mm
- Feels heavier in the head
- More power, especially on overheads
- Less manoeuvrable
- Typical of diamond rackets

The combination of weight and balance creates the "swing weight," which is what you actually feel when you swing the racket. A light racket with a head-heavy balance can feel similar to a heavier racket with an even balance.

## Core materials: EVA vs FOAM

The core is the foam or rubber material sandwiched between the two hitting surfaces. The primary determinant of how the racket feels on contact.

### EVA (ethylene-vinyl acetate)

EVA is a dense, rubber-like material available in various densities.

**Soft EVA**

- Softer feel on contact
- More power (trampoline effect)
- Less control at high swing speeds
- More comfortable for extended play
- Good for beginners and intermediate players

**Medium EVA**

- Balanced feel
- Good combination of power and control
- The most common core material
- Suitable for all levels

**Hard EVA (Black EVA / Ultra EVA)**

- Firm feel on contact
- Maximum control and precision
- Less inherent power (you generate your own)
- Preferred by advanced and professional players
- More demanding on the arm

### FOAM (polyethylene foam)

FOAM cores are softer and less dense than EVA.

- Very soft, comfortable feel
- High power output with less effort
- Less control and precision than EVA
- Excellent for beginners
- Good for players with arm problems
- Less durable than EVA (may lose responsiveness over time)

### Hybrid cores

Many modern rackets use a combination of EVA and FOAM layers, placing softer material in the centre and firmer material near the edges, or the reverse. Hybrids try to deliver the best of both.

## Face materials: carbon fiber vs fiberglass

The face is the outermost layer that contacts the ball. The two primary materials offer distinctly different playing characteristics.

### Carbon fiber

- **Firm feel** with crisp ball contact
- **More power** at high swing speeds
- **Greater durability** against wear and tear
- **Better for spin** generation (especially rough carbon textures)
- **Less comfortable** on off-centre hits (transmits more vibration)
- **Higher price**
- Preferred by advanced and professional players

Carbon fiber comes in various weaves and thicknesses. Common types include 3K (3,000 fibres per tow), 12K (12,000 fibres per tow), and 18K (18,000 fibres per tow). Higher K numbers generally indicate stiffer surfaces.

### Fiberglass

- **Softer feel** with more flex on contact
- **More forgiving** on off-centre hits
- **Greater comfort** and vibration absorption
- **Less power** at high swing speeds
- **Lower durability** than carbon
- **Lower price**
- Ideal for beginners and intermediate players

### Composite surfaces

Many rackets use a combination of carbon fiber and fiberglass. A common configuration is carbon fiber on the primary hitting side and fiberglass on the other, or a blend of both materials across the surface.

### Textured surfaces

Some rackets feature rough or textured surface treatments designed to increase spin.

- **Rough carbon** surfaces grab the ball more effectively, allowing more topspin and slice.
- **3D textures** or raised patterns can increase spin but may wear down over time.
- Surface roughness regulations are governed by the FIP, which sets limits on how textured a racket face can be.

## Grip size and replacement

### Grip sizes

Padel rackets typically come in one or two grip sizes. The standard grip circumference ranges from about 105 mm to 115 mm. Unlike tennis, padel offers less variation.

### Overgrips

Most players add one or two overgrips to customise the feel.

- **Thin overgrip (0.5 mm).** Adds minimal size, improves tackiness.
- **Standard overgrip (0.6–0.75 mm).** Moderate size increase, good cushioning.
- **Thick overgrip (1.0 mm+).** Significant size increase, maximum cushioning.

Replace overgrips regularly, especially in hot or humid conditions. A worn, slippery grip compromises control and leads to wrist strain from gripping too tightly.

## Choosing by skill level

### Complete beginners

- **Shape:** round
- **Weight:** light to medium (340–360 g)
- **Balance:** head-light to even
- **Core:** soft EVA or FOAM
- **Surface:** fiberglass or composite
- **Budget:** entry-level (€50–€100)

### Improving intermediates

- **Shape:** round or teardrop
- **Weight:** medium (355–370 g)
- **Balance:** even
- **Core:** medium EVA
- **Surface:** carbon/fiberglass composite
- **Budget:** mid-range (€100–€200)

### Advanced players

- **Shape:** teardrop or diamond (depending on style)
- **Weight:** medium to heavy (365–385 g)
- **Balance:** even to head-heavy
- **Core:** hard EVA or hybrid
- **Surface:** carbon fiber
- **Budget:** premium (€200–€350)

### Professional players

- **Shape:** varies by role and style
- **Weight:** customised with added weights
- **Balance:** precisely tuned
- **Core:** professional-grade EVA
- **Surface:** high-K carbon fiber with texture
- **Budget:** sponsorship or top-end (€300+)

## Common mistakes

**Buying too much racket.** The most common beginner error: choosing an advanced diamond-shaped, head-heavy racket because it sounds better. It plays worse in beginner hands. Start with a forgiving round racket and upgrade as your technique improves.

**Ignoring weight.** A heavy racket in the hands of a player without the conditioning or technique to handle it leads to elbow and wrist injuries. When in doubt, go lighter.

**Chasing brand names.** The most expensive racket from the most popular brand is not necessarily the best racket for you. A mid-range round racket that fits your game will outperform a premium diamond racket that doesn't.

**Not testing before buying.** If your club or local shop offers demo rackets, use them. Even 15 minutes of play with a racket tells you more than any review.

**Forgetting about the grip.** An otherwise perfect racket with the wrong grip size or a worn-out grip becomes uncomfortable and unreliable. Invest in good overgrips and replace them regularly.

## When to replace your racket

Padel rackets do not last forever. Signs it's time for a new one:

- Cracks in the surface (compromises structural integrity)
- Dead spots where the ball doesn't come off the racket as expected
- Loss of pop (the core has compressed and lost responsiveness)
- Frame damage from wall or ground contact

For a regular player, a racket typically lasts 12–18 months before performance noticeably degrades. Competitive players who play frequently go through rackets faster.

The single best piece of advice for choosing a racket: prioritise control and comfort over power, especially when starting out. A racket that helps you keep the ball in play consistently will improve your game far more than one that hits harder but sends every other shot into the glass. Start with a round shape, medium weight, and soft-to-medium core. As your technique develops and you understand your playing style, make more specialised choices.
