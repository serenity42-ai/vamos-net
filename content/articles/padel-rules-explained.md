# Padel Rules Explained: Everything You Need to Know

Padel scoring is identical to tennis. So is the basic rally structure. Where padel is its own sport is in three places: how you serve (always underarm), how the walls factor into play (after the bounce, anything goes), and what happens when the ball leaves the court (you can chase it through the door). Get those three things right and the rest of the rule book follows.

## Scoring

Same as tennis, point for point.

### Points within a game

- First point: 15
- Second point: 30
- Third point: 40
- Fourth point: game

When both pairs reach 40 (deuce), the next point gives the scoring team "advantage." Win the next point and the game is yours. Lose it and you're back to deuce. Continues until one team wins two consecutive points from deuce.

Some tournaments and social formats use **golden point** (*punto de oro*) instead. At deuce, the receiving pair chooses which side the serve is delivered to, and the next point decides the game outright. Speeds up play and adds pressure to high-stakes moments.

### Sets

A set goes to the first pair to win six games with at least a two-game lead. At 6–6, a tiebreak is played.

In a tiebreak, points count 1, 2, 3 and so on (not 15, 30, 40). The first pair to seven points with a two-point lead wins the tiebreak and the set. Service alternates every two points; players switch ends every six points.

### Matches

Most padel matches are best of three sets. Professional tournaments use this format for every round including finals. Some recreational leagues run shorter formats: a single set, or pro-set / tiebreak-only.

## Serving

The serve is where padel most visibly departs from tennis. There is no overhead smash to open the point. The serve is underarm, and the rules around it are precise.

### How to serve

1. Stand behind the service line, between the center line and the side wall.
2. Bounce the ball on the ground (drop or throw, then let it bounce once).
3. Strike the ball at or below waist height.
4. At least one foot must be on the ground at contact.
5. The ball must cross the net and land in the diagonal service box.

### Service faults

A serve is a fault if:

- The ball does not land in the correct service box
- The server strikes the ball above waist height
- The server steps on or over the service line before contact
- The server misses the ball entirely (yes, that counts)
- Both feet leave the ground during the serve

Two attempts per point, like tennis. A double fault loses the point.

### The let rule

A served ball that clips the net and lands in the correct service box is a let, replayed. Standard.

But padel has a second let rule that catches every beginner: if the served ball bounces in the correct box and then hits the **side wall** (mesh or glass on the side of the receiver's court) before the second bounce, that's also a let.

If the served ball lands in the correct box and then hits the **back wall** (the glass behind the receiver), it is a valid serve and the receiver must play it. The distinction between side wall (let) and back wall (in play) on the serve is one of the most commonly misunderstood rules in padel.

### Service rotation

Service rotates among all four players, like doubles tennis. The pair that serves first decides which player opens the set; the opposing pair decides their order when their turn comes. Order is fixed for the set. New order may be set at the start of each new set.

## Rally play and wall rules

Once the serve is in, the rules around the rally are where padel's character emerges.

### The wall rule, in one line

After the ball bounces on the floor on your side, it can hit any wall, in any sequence, and you can still play it. The only requirement: it must bounce on the floor first. You cannot play a ball that hits the wall before touching your floor.

### Volleys

Volley away. Volleys are central to padel strategy, especially at the net. The only restriction: the receiver must let the serve bounce before playing it (no volleying the serve).

### Playing off the walls

After the ball bounces, the sequence of wall contacts doesn't matter. Bounce, back wall, side wall, then your shot. As long as the ball hasn't bounced on the floor twice, it's still live. This is what creates padel's signature retrievals.

### Leaving the court

Padel's most dramatic rule. If a ball is lobbed high enough that it sails over your back wall or side fencing and out of the enclosure, you are allowed to leave the court through the side openings, play the ball outside, and return it back over the wall into the opponents' court.

It looks impossible. It happens regularly in pro padel. Sprinting outside the court to whip a ball back over the glass is one of the sights you only see in this sport.

### Net rules

- If the ball hits the net during a rally (not the serve) and goes over, play continues
- You may not touch the net with your body, racket, or clothing during the point
- You may not reach over the net to hit the ball; the ball must have crossed to your side first

## Court boundaries and out-of-play situations

The walls add a third dimension that you have to think about differently from most racket sports.

### In play

- Any ball that bounces on the floor within the court lines, regardless of which walls it then hits, is in play
- A ball that hits the metal mesh or glass after bouncing is in play
- A ball that goes over the back wall or fencing and is retrieved from outside is in play

### Out of play

A ball is out (and the point is lost) if:

- It bounces on the floor outside the court lines before hitting a wall
- It hits a permanent fixture above the legal playing area (lights, ceiling beams) before bouncing on the opponents' floor; indoor venues vary on ceiling rules
- It gets stuck in the mesh or any part of the court structure
- It passes through a gap in the fencing rather than over it

### The double-bounce rule

If the ball bounces twice on the floor on one side before being returned, the other team wins the point. The walls don't enter into this; only floor bounces count. Multiple wall contacts between the first and second floor bounces are legal.

## Doubles format and positioning

Padel is almost exclusively a doubles game. Singles padel exists, played on a narrower 20×6 m court, but the standard game requires four players.

### Positioning

There is no rule dictating where players must stand during a rally (aside from server and receiver positions during the serve). But the strategy is well-developed:

- **Net position (attacking):** both partners side by side near the net. The dominant position in padel; volleys and pressure live here.
- **Back position (defending):** both partners near the back wall, ready to retrieve deep shots and lobs. The work from here is to use lobs, passing shots, and well-placed groundstrokes to climb back to the net.
- **Mixed positioning** (one up, one back) is generally weak. It happens during transitions but you don't choose it.

### Communication

Since each partner covers half the court, calling shots ("mine," "yours," "leave it") matters. Especially on balls travelling through the middle or coming off walls at unexpected angles.

## Common misconceptions

### "You can hit the walls on the other side directly"

No. Your shot must clear the net and bounce on the opponents' floor before it touches any wall on their side. A direct smash into the opponents' glass without a floor bounce loses the point.

### "The serve can bounce off the side wall and still be good"

No. If the served ball lands in the correct service box and then hits the side wall before the second bounce, it's a let and replayed. Side-wall contact during a rally is fine; on the serve, it's a let.

### "Padel scoring is different from tennis"

It isn't. 15, 30, 40, deuce, advantage, game, set, match. The only variation is the optional golden point rule at deuce, which some tournaments and social formats adopt.

### "You can only play the ball off the walls on your side"

Mostly true during a rally. The exception is the door rule: when a lob sails over the back wall or fencing, you can leave the court and play the ball outside, returning it over the wall.

### "If the ball hits the fence, it's out"

Not necessarily. The mesh fencing is part of the court. A ball that bounces on the floor and then hits the fence is still in play. What ends the point is the second floor bounce, not wall contact.

## Changes of ends

Players switch ends after every odd-numbered game (after games 1, 3, 5, and so on). In tiebreaks, switch ends every six points.

## Time between points and games

Recreational padel is mostly self-paced, but official rules apply in tournaments:

- **Between points:** the server should serve within roughly 20–25 seconds
- **At changeovers:** about 90 seconds
- **Between sets:** up to 120 seconds

## Refereeing

Most recreational padel is self-officiated. Players call their own lines and resolve disputes by agreement, which generally works because padel culture leans heavily toward sportsmanship.

Professional and high-level competitive matches use a chair umpire, with electronic line-calling increasingly being deployed in the same way as in tennis.

## Etiquette

Not rules but expected:

- Call the ball "out" immediately and clearly if it lands outside the lines on your side
- Acknowledge good shots from opponents (a nod, a tap of the racket)
- Skip the excessive celebration and intimidation
- Return stray balls to neighbouring courts promptly
- Warm up together before the match starts

That's the whole rule book. Ten minutes to learn, a season to internalise, a career to master at the level the pros play. The rules are simple. The game they create is not. Book a court.
