# Padel Rules Explained: Everything You Need to Know

Understanding padel's rules is straightforward, especially if you have any familiarity with tennis. The scoring system is identical, the basic framework of serves and rallies is similar, and the objective -- win more points than your opponents -- is unchanged. Where padel diverges is in the details: how you serve, how the walls factor into play, and how the enclosed court creates unique situations that no other racket sport shares.

This guide covers every rule you need to know, whether you are stepping onto a padel court for the first time or settling a debate with your regular playing partners.

## The Scoring System

Padel uses the same scoring system as tennis, point for point.

### Points Within a Game

- First point: 15
- Second point: 30
- Third point: 40
- Fourth point: Game

When both pairs reach 40 (deuce), the next point gives the scoring team "advantage." If the team with advantage wins the next point, they win the game. If they lose it, the score returns to deuce. This continues until one team wins two consecutive points from deuce.

Some tournaments and social formats use "golden point" (also called "punto de oro") instead of advantage scoring. Under this rule, at deuce the receiving pair chooses which side the serve is delivered to, and the next point decides the game outright. This speeds up play and adds pressure to crucial moments.

### Sets

A set is won by the first pair to win six games, provided they lead by at least two games. If the score reaches 6-6, a tiebreak is played.

In a tiebreak, points are counted 1, 2, 3, and so on (rather than 15, 30, 40). The first pair to reach seven points with a two-point lead wins the tiebreak and the set. Service alternates every two points in the tiebreak, and players switch ends after every six points.

### Matches

Most padel matches are best of three sets. In professional tournaments, all rounds including finals follow this format. Some recreational leagues may use shorter formats such as a single set or a tiebreak-only system.

## Serving Rules

The serve is where padel most visibly departs from tennis. There is no overhead smash to open the point. The serve in padel is underarm, and the rules governing it are precise.

### How to Serve

1. The server must stand behind the service line, between the center line and the side wall
2. The ball must be bounced on the ground. The server drops or throws the ball and lets it bounce once
3. The racket must make contact with the ball at or below waist height
4. At the moment of contact, the server must have at least one foot on the ground
5. The ball must cross the net and land in the diagonally opposite service box

### Service Faults

A serve is a fault if:

- The ball does not land in the correct service box
- The server strikes the ball above waist height
- The server steps on or over the service line before contact
- The server misses the ball entirely (yes, this counts as a fault)
- Both feet leave the ground during the serve

As in tennis, the server gets two attempts. A double fault results in the loss of the point.

### The Let Rule

A served ball that clips the net and lands in the correct service box is normally a let and is replayed. However, padel has an additional let rule that catches many beginners off guard: if the served ball bounces in the correct box and then hits the side wall (the metal mesh or glass on the side of the receiver's court) before the second bounce, it is also a let.

If the served ball lands in the correct box and then hits the back wall (the glass behind the receiver), it is a valid serve and the receiver must play it. This distinction between side wall (let) and back wall (in play) on the serve is one of the most commonly misunderstood rules in padel.

### Service Rotation

The serving order rotates among all four players, just as in doubles tennis. The pair that serves first decides which player serves the opening game. The opposing pair decides their order when their turn comes. This rotation remains fixed for the entire set. A new serving order may be established at the start of a new set.

## Rally Play and Wall Rules

Once the serve is in play, the rules governing rallies are where padel's unique character emerges.

### The Fundamental Rule of Wall Play

After the ball bounces on the floor on your side of the court, it may hit any wall -- back glass, side glass, metal mesh -- and you can still play it. The key requirement is that the ball must bounce on the floor first. You cannot play a ball that hits the wall before touching the ground on your side (this would be the equivalent of a volley off the wall, which is not allowed).

### Volleys

You can volley the ball (hit it before it bounces) at any point during a rally, just as in tennis. Volleys are a central part of padel strategy, especially at the net. The restriction on volleys applies only to the return of serve -- the receiver must let the serve bounce before playing it.

### Playing the Ball Off the Walls

After the ball bounces, the sequence of wall contacts does not matter. The ball might bounce, hit the back wall, and then the side wall before you strike it. As long as it has not bounced on the floor a second time, it remains in play. This creates extraordinary retrievals and is one of the most exciting aspects of padel.

### The Ball Leaving the Court

One of padel's most spectacular rules: if the ball is lobbed over your head and goes high enough, it may sail over the back wall or side fencing and leave the court entirely. When this happens, the defending player is allowed to leave the court through the side openings (the doors or gaps in the fencing) and play the ball outside the enclosure, returning it over the wall or fencing and back into the opponents' court.

This rule produces some of the most dramatic moments in professional padel. Players sprinting outside the court to retrieve a lob and whipping the ball back over the glass is a sight unique to this sport.

### Net Rules

- If the ball hits the net and goes over during a rally (not a serve), play continues
- Players may not touch the net with their body, racket, or clothing during the point
- Players may not reach over the net to hit the ball -- the ball must have crossed to your side before you can strike it

## Court Boundaries and Out-of-Play Situations

Understanding what is "in" and "out" in padel requires thinking differently than in most racket sports, because the walls create a third dimension.

### In Play

- Any ball that bounces on the floor within the court lines, regardless of which walls it subsequently contacts, is in play
- A ball that hits the metal mesh or glass after bouncing on the floor is in play
- A ball that goes over the back wall or fencing (and remains retrievable from outside) is in play

### Out of Play

A ball is out of play (and the point is lost) if:

- It bounces on the floor outside the court lines before hitting a wall
- It hits any permanent fixture above the legal playing area (lights, ceiling beams, etc.) before bouncing on the floor on the opponents' side -- in indoor facilities, ceiling height rules vary by venue
- It gets stuck in the metal mesh fencing or any part of the court structure
- It passes through any gap in the fencing (rather than over it)

### The Double Bounce Rule

As in all racket sports, if the ball bounces twice on the floor on one side before being returned, the point goes to the other team. Given padel's wall play, the ball may contact multiple walls between its first and second floor bounces -- all of this is legal. Only the floor bounces count.

## Doubles Format and Positioning

Padel is almost exclusively played as doubles. While singles padel exists (played on a narrower court, 20 meters by 6 meters), the standard game requires four players.

### Positioning

There is no rule dictating where players must stand during a rally (aside from the server and receiver positions during the serve). However, padel strategy has developed clear positional principles:

- **Net position (attacking):** Both players from the attacking team position themselves close to the net, side by side. This is the dominant position in padel, allowing players to volley and put pressure on opponents.
- **Back position (defending):** Both players from the defending team position themselves near the back wall, ready to retrieve deep shots and lobs. From here, the goal is to work back to the net position through a combination of lobs, passing shots, and well-placed groundstrokes.
- **Mixed positioning:** One player at the net and one at the back is generally considered a weak formation, though it occurs naturally during transitions.

### Communication

Since partners cover half the court each, communication is critical. Players call shots ("mine," "yours," "leave it") to avoid confusion, especially on balls that travel through the center of the court or bounce off walls at unexpected angles.

## Common Misconceptions

### "You can hit the walls on the other side directly"

No. When you hit the ball, it must travel over the net and bounce on the floor of the opponents' court before it can contact any wall on their side. You cannot smash the ball directly into the opponents' glass -- it must bounce first. If your shot hits the opponents' wall before bouncing on their floor, you lose the point.

### "The serve can bounce off the side wall and still be good"

No. If the served ball bounces in the correct service box and then contacts the side wall before the second bounce, it is a let and must be replayed. This is unique to the serve. During rallies, side wall contact after a floor bounce is perfectly fine.

### "Padel scoring is different from tennis"

It is not. The scoring is identical: 15, 30, 40, deuce, advantage, game, set, match. The only variation is the optional golden point rule at deuce, which some tournaments and social formats adopt.

### "You can only play the ball off the walls on your side"

This is mostly true during a rally, but the key exception is the rule allowing players to leave the court to retrieve a ball that has gone over the walls. In that scenario, you are playing the ball outside the court and hitting it back over the wall into the opponents' side.

### "If the ball hits the fence, it's out"

Not necessarily. The metal mesh fencing is part of the court. If the ball bounces on the floor and then hits the fencing, it is still in play. What matters is whether the ball has bounced twice on the floor.

## Changes of Ends

Players switch ends after every odd-numbered game (after games 1, 3, 5, and so on). During tiebreaks, players switch ends after every six points.

## Time Between Points and Games

While recreational padel is typically self-paced, official rules govern rest periods:

- **Between points:** The server should serve within a reasonable time, typically 20-25 seconds
- **At changeovers:** Players are allowed a short rest when switching ends, typically 90 seconds
- **Between sets:** A break of up to 120 seconds is standard

## Referee Decisions and Self-Officiation

Most recreational padel is self-officiated. Players call their own lines and resolve disputes through mutual agreement. This works well because padel culture tends to emphasize sportsmanship and fair play.

In professional and competitive settings, matches are officiated by a chair umpire, and increasingly by electronic line-calling systems similar to those used in tennis.

## A Note on Padel Etiquette

While not strictly rules, certain conventions are expected on the padel court:

- Call the ball "out" immediately and clearly if it lands outside the lines on your side
- Acknowledge good shots from opponents -- a quick nod or tap of the racket
- Avoid excessive celebration or intimidation
- Return stray balls to neighboring courts promptly
- Warm up together before the match begins

## Putting It All Together

Padel's rules are designed to create a sport that is accessible, social, and endlessly engaging. The tennis-based scoring system is familiar. The underarm serve levels the playing field. The wall play adds a dimension that rewards creativity and persistence over raw power. And the doubles format makes every point a team effort.

The rules are simple enough that you can learn them in ten minutes and play a coherent game your first time on court. But the strategic depth they create -- the angles, the positioning, the wall play, the partnership dynamics -- is what keeps players coming back session after session, year after year.

Now that you know the rules, there is only one thing left to do: book a court.
