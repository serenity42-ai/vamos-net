# What is Padel? The Complete Guide to the World's Fastest-Growing Sport

Padel was invented in a backyard in Acapulco in 1969. Fifty-seven years later, 35 million people in 150 countries play it. The International Padel Federation counts roughly 70,000 courts in operation or under construction worldwide. Spain alone has nearly 17,000 of them.

This guide covers what padel is, how it's played, why it's spreading at this speed, and how to get started.

## The origins

Enrique Corcuera, a Mexican businessman, adapted a squash court at his Acapulco home in 1969 by adding glass and metal walls and elements of platform tennis. He called the result "Paddle Corcuera." The defining innovation was that the walls were in play: balls could be struck off the glass after bouncing on the floor, opening up a strategic dimension no other racket sport had.

The sport might have stayed a private curiosity if Alfonso de Hohenlohe, a Spanish aristocrat, had not visited Acapulco in the early 1970s and brought it back to Marbella. The Marbella Club crowd took to it. From there, padel filtered through Spanish high society into the mainstream.

Argentina imported the game in the mid-1970s and turned it into a national obsession. Today Spain and Argentina remain the twin heartlands of professional padel.

The FIP was founded in 1991 with four member federations. It now governs more than 100. Olympic inclusion is a serious conversation, not a distant aspiration.

## How padel is played

Padel is a doubles game. Singles padel exists but barely; the sport is built for four players, two per side, and almost everyone plays it that way. That is also one of the reasons it spread so fast: book a court with three friends, and you have a social evening built in.

Players use solid, perforated rackets called *palas*, made from carbon fiber or fiberglass with a foam core. The balls look like tennis balls with slightly less pressure, which makes them slower and easier to control. Rallies start with an underarm serve: the server bounces the ball behind the service line, then strikes it at or below waist height into the diagonal service box. From there it plays much like tennis. With one structural difference.

### The walls are in play

After the ball bounces on the floor, it can hit any wall on the receiving side and the rally continues. This single rule changes the sport's geometry. Shots that would be tennis winners are retrievable; rallies last longer; defenders have a second chance at every ball that flies past the baseline. Players develop a vocabulary that doesn't exist in tennis: the *bajada* off the back wall, the *víbora* smash that kicks off the side glass, the *bandeja* overhead used as a tactical reset rather than a finishing blow.

The walls also make padel easier for beginners. A miscued shot in tennis is gone. A miscued shot in padel often comes back into play. The same rule that creates depth for advanced players reduces frustration for newcomers.

## The padel court

A standard padel court is 20 meters long by 10 meters wide (about 66 by 33 feet), roughly a quarter the size of a tennis court. The court is enclosed on all four sides by a combination of glass panels and metal mesh.

### Court structure

- **Back walls:** 4 meters of glass, sometimes topped with mesh up to a total height of 4 meters
- **Side walls:** glass at the back (3 meters) transitioning to mesh further forward
- **Net:** 88 cm at center, 92 cm at the posts
- **Service lines:** 6.95 meters from the net on each side
- **Surface:** typically artificial turf with sand infill, though concrete and acrylic versions exist

The compact footprint matters. A padel court fits where a tennis court doesn't: rooftops, narrow urban lots, repurposed warehouses. That is one of the structural reasons the sport is expanding so rapidly in dense European and Middle Eastern cities.

## Basic rules

Padel scoring is identical to tennis. Points run 15, 30, 40, game. Deuce and advantage apply. Matches are best of three sets, each set won by the first pair to reach six games with a two-game lead. Tiebreaks at 6–6.

### Serving

- The server stands behind the service line, bounces the ball, and strikes it underarm at or below waist level
- The serve must land in the diagonal service box
- If the served ball clips the net and lands correctly but then touches a side or back wall before the second bounce, it is a let and is replayed
- Two attempts per point, like tennis

### Returning and rally play

- The ball must bounce on the floor before being returned (no volleying the serve)
- After the bounce, it can hit any wall and stay in play
- Players may strike the ball before or after it touches a wall, as long as it has hit the floor first
- The return must clear the net and land in the opponents' court (floor first)
- Players may exit the court through the side openings to retrieve a lob, one of the more spectacular plays in padel

### Winning points

Points are won as in tennis: when the ball bounces twice on the opponents' side, when they hit it into the net or out of the enclosed area, or when they commit a fault.

## Why padel is the fastest-growing sport

The numbers are what they are. According to the FIP World Padel Report (late 2025): 35 million players globally, 16.1% growth in clubs year on year, 15.2% growth in courts, and 42% growth in federation membership in a single year.

Four things explain the curve.

### Accessibility

Padel is easier to start than tennis. Smaller court, slower ball, underarm serve. Beginners get into competitive rallies in their first session, which is the threshold below which most racket sports lose new players. Walls keep the ball in play. Doubles keeps it social.

### Easy floor, high ceiling

Easy to start does not mean easy to master. Advanced padel involves reading spin off glass, anticipating wall angles, choreographing position with a partner, and an arsenal of specialised shots. Top-tier professional padel, on display every week on the Premier Padel tour, is fast and tactically dense.

### It fits cities

Roughly 200 square meters per court. Courts stack. They go on rooftops. They squeeze into urban lots where a tennis facility is impossible. Operators have noticed; padel facility development is one of the fastest-growing real-estate niches in Europe and the Gulf.

### Money and celebrity

Lionel Messi plays padel. So do Neymar and David Beckham. The Qatar Investment Authority is the principal backer of Premier Padel. Private equity has been flowing into padel club operators since 2022. The combination of celebrity validation and serious capital has accelerated infrastructure that would otherwise have taken a decade.

## Where padel is most popular

### Spain

Padel's undisputed capital. Nearly 17,000 courts. Second most popular participation sport in the country, behind football. Court time is regularly booked days in advance.

### Argentina

Over 7,000 courts. A deep competitive culture that has produced many of the world's top professionals on both the men's and women's tours. Played everywhere from private clubs to public parks.

### Italy

Roughly 9,700 courts, almost all built in the last five years. Among the fastest-growing markets in Europe.

### Rest of Europe

Sweden, the Netherlands, and the United Kingdom have grown explosively. UK participation more than tripled between 2023 and 2024, reaching over 400,000 players. France and Portugal have each surpassed 1,000 courts.

### Middle East, Asia, and the Americas

The UAE, Saudi Arabia, and Qatar are investing aggressively, often as part of national sport-diversification strategies. The United States is in early-stage growth, concentrated in Miami, New York, Los Angeles, and Dallas. Japan, India, and China are the next frontier.

## The professional game

Professional padel runs primarily through the Premier Padel tour, sanctioned by the FIP and backed by the Qatar Investment Authority. The tour visits cities across Europe, the Middle East, and the Americas, with prize money and broadcast reach growing each season.

On the men's side, Agustín Tapia and Arturo Coello entered the 2026 season as the top-ranked pair, known for explosive shot-making. Alejandro Galán, who won the 2025 World Padel Championship in Kuwait alongside Fede Chingotto, remains one of the sport's biggest names. Other top players include Juan Lebrón and Paquito Navarro.

In the women's game, Gemma Triay and Delfina Brea topped the 2025 final rankings. Ariana Sánchez and Paula Josemaría are close behind. The women's tour has grown sharply in depth since Premier Padel introduced equal prize money for women's events.

## How to get started

Padel costs less to enter than almost any other racket sport.

### What you need

- **A padel racket:** beginner rackets run €50–€150. Round-shaped models with a large sweet spot are ideal for newcomers.
- **Balls:** like tennis balls with slightly less pressure. A can of three is a few euros.
- **Shoes:** any flat-soled court shoe or tennis shoe. Avoid running shoes with thick, curved soles.
- **Clothing:** standard athletic gear.

### Finding courts and partners

The easiest path is the Playtomic app, which lists available courts in most padel-active cities and helps you find opponents at your level. Most facilities offer Americano sessions — social round-robins where you rotate partners every few games, making it easy to meet other players.

### Taking lessons

Two or three lessons accelerate your first six months meaningfully. A coach gets your grip, your serve, and your ball-off-the-glass instincts to a place that takes a beginner months to find alone.

### Join a community

Padel is social by design. Local clubs, WhatsApp groups, and organized leagues make it straightforward to fold the sport into your week. Most cities with active padel scenes have weekly social games and ladder competitions.

## The future

Padel's trajectory does not look like it is plateauing. 35 million players, 70,000 courts projected by 2026, Olympic inclusion under serious discussion, the professional tour maturing on the back of Qatari investment, and new geographies opening every year. The transition from niche curiosity to global mainstream is not in question; only the timeline.

For anyone who hasn't tried it: book a court, find three friends, and play.
