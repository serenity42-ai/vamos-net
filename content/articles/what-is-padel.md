# What is Padel? The Complete Guide to the World's Fastest-Growing Sport

Padel is a racket sport that combines elements of tennis, squash, and racquetball into something entirely its own. Played in doubles on an enclosed court roughly one-third the size of a tennis court, padel has quietly become one of the fastest-growing sports on the planet. The International Padel Federation (FIP) now counts over 35 million players worldwide across more than 150 countries, with an estimated 70,000 courts in operation or under construction globally.

If you have never heard of padel, you are not alone -- at least not in the English-speaking world. But that is changing fast. From London to Los Angeles, new padel facilities are opening at a pace that few could have predicted just a decade ago. This guide covers everything you need to know about the sport: where it came from, how it works, why it is spreading so rapidly, and how you can get started.

## The Origins of Padel

Padel was invented in 1969 by Enrique Corcuera, a Mexican businessman, at his home in Acapulco. Corcuera adapted a squash court on his property by incorporating elements of platform tennis, creating what he called "Paddle Corcuera." The key innovation was the use of walls as part of the playing field -- balls could be played off the glass and metal enclosures surrounding the court, adding a strategic dimension that no other racket sport offered.

The sport might have remained a backyard curiosity if not for Alfonso de Hohenlohe, a Spanish aristocrat and friend of Corcuera. After visiting Acapulco and falling in love with the game, Hohenlohe brought padel to Marbella, Spain, in the early 1970s. From his exclusive Marbella Club, the sport spread through Spanish high society before filtering into the broader population.

Argentina picked up the sport next, importing it in the mid-1970s. The Argentine passion for padel proved even more intense than Spain's, and the country quickly developed a thriving competitive scene. These two nations -- Spain and Argentina -- remain the twin heartlands of professional padel to this day.

The FIP was founded in 1991 with just four member nations. Today it governs the sport across more than 100 national federations, and padel's inclusion in the Olympic programme is now a serious topic of conversation rather than a distant dream.

## How Padel is Played

At its core, padel is a doubles game. While singles padel exists, the sport is designed for four players on court -- two per side. This makes padel inherently social, one of the key reasons behind its popularity.

Players use solid, perforated rackets (often called "padel rackets" or "palas") rather than strung rackets. These are typically made from carbon fiber or fiberglass with a foam core, and they are considerably shorter than tennis rackets. The ball is similar to a tennis ball but with slightly less pressure, making it slower and easier to control.

The game begins with an underarm serve. The server must bounce the ball behind the service line and strike it at or below waist height, directing it diagonally into the opponent's service box. After the serve, the rally continues much like tennis -- but with one crucial difference.

### The Walls Are in Play

The defining feature of padel is that the surrounding walls are part of the game. After the ball bounces on the floor, it can hit any wall on the receiving side, and players may still return it. This wall play transforms the sport. Shots that would be winners in tennis become retrievable, creating longer rallies and more dynamic points. Players develop an entirely new vocabulary of shots -- the "bajada" off the back wall, the "vibora" smash that kicks off the side glass, and the "bandeja" overhead played as a tactical reset rather than a finishing blow.

The walls also eliminate dead space. In tennis, a ball that flies past the baseline is gone. In padel, that same ball bounces off the back glass and returns into play, giving the defending team another chance. This makes padel more forgiving for beginners and more strategically complex for advanced players.

## The Padel Court: Dimensions and Layout

A standard padel court measures 20 meters long by 10 meters wide (approximately 66 feet by 33 feet), making it roughly 25 percent the size of a tennis court. The court is enclosed on all sides by a combination of glass panels and metal mesh fencing.

### Court Structure

- **Back walls:** 4 meters of glass, sometimes topped with metal mesh up to a total height of 4 meters
- **Side walls:** A combination of glass (3 meters high near the back) and metal mesh extending along the sides
- **Net:** The net sits at the center, measuring 88 centimeters (about 34.6 inches) at the center and rising to 92 centimeters (about 36.2 inches) at the posts
- **Service lines:** Located 6.95 meters from the net on each side
- **Playing surface:** Typically artificial turf with sand infill, though concrete and other surfaces exist

The enclosed design means padel courts have a relatively small footprint, making them easier to install than tennis courts. They can be built indoors, outdoors, or on rooftops -- a significant factor in the sport's urban expansion.

## Basic Rules of Padel

Padel's scoring system is identical to tennis: points progress through 15, 30, 40, and game, with deuce and advantage rules applying. Matches are typically best of three sets, with each set won by the first pair to reach six games with a two-game lead. Tiebreaks are used at 6-6.

### Serving

- The server must stand behind the service line and bounce the ball on the ground before striking it
- The serve must be underarm, with contact made at or below waist level
- The ball must land in the diagonal service box
- If the served ball hits the net and lands in the correct box but then touches the side or back wall before the second bounce, it is a let and must be replayed
- Each server gets two attempts, just as in tennis

### Returning and Rally Play

- The ball must bounce once on the floor before being returned (no volleying the serve)
- After the bounce, the ball may hit any wall and still be played
- Players may hit the ball before or after it contacts a wall, as long as it has bounced on the floor first
- The ball must clear the net and land in the opponents' court area (floor first) on the return
- Players may leave the court through the side openings to retrieve a lob -- one of the most spectacular plays in padel

### Winning Points

Points are won in the same manner as tennis: when the ball bounces twice on the opponents' side, when the opponents hit the ball into the net or out of the enclosed area, or when the opponents commit a fault.

## Why Padel is the Fastest-Growing Sport in the World

The numbers tell a compelling story. According to the FIP World Padel Report published in late 2025, the sport now boasts over 35 million players globally, with a 16.1 percent increase in clubs and a 15.2 percent rise in courts compared to the previous year. Federation membership grew by 42 percent in a single year.

Several factors drive this extraordinary growth.

### Accessibility and Social Appeal

Padel is easier to pick up than tennis. The smaller court, slower ball, and underarm serve mean that complete beginners can enjoy competitive rallies within their first session. The enclosed walls keep the ball in play longer, reducing frustration and increasing the fun factor. And because padel is played in doubles, it is inherently social -- four friends can book a court and have a genuinely good time regardless of skill level.

### The Learning Curve is Gentle, the Ceiling is High

While padel is easy to start, it offers enormous depth. Advanced play involves reading spin off glass, mastering wall angles, coordinating positioning with a partner, and developing an arsenal of specialized shots. Professional padel, showcased on the Premier Padel tour, is breathtaking in its speed, athleticism, and tactical complexity.

### It Fits Urban Spaces

A padel court requires roughly 200 square meters -- about a quarter of a tennis court. Courts can be stacked, built on rooftops, or squeezed into urban lots. This makes padel viable in dense cities where land is expensive and space is scarce. Entrepreneurs and investors have noticed: padel facility development is booming in European and Middle Eastern cities.

### Celebrity and Investment

High-profile athletes and investors have poured money into padel. Football stars like Lionel Messi, Neymar, and David Beckham have been photographed playing padel, bringing mainstream attention. The Qatar Investment Authority's backing of Premier Padel and significant private equity flowing into padel facilities have professionalized the sport's infrastructure.

## Where Padel is Most Popular

### Spain

Spain is padel's undisputed capital, with nearly 17,000 courts and millions of regular players. It is the second most popular participation sport in the country, behind only football. Every major Spanish city has dozens of padel clubs, and court time is often booked days in advance.

### Argentina

Argentina has over 7,000 courts and a deep competitive culture. Many of the world's top professional players, both men and women, come from Argentina. The sport is woven into the social fabric, played in clubs, parks, and private facilities across the country.

### Italy

Italy has embraced padel with characteristic enthusiasm, building over 9,700 courts in recent years. The sport's growth in Italy has been among the fastest in Europe, driven by its social nature and compatibility with Italian sporting culture.

### Rest of Europe

Sweden, the Netherlands, and the United Kingdom have experienced explosive growth. The UK saw participation more than triple between 2023 and 2024, reaching over 400,000 players. France and Portugal have also seen substantial expansion, each surpassing 1,000 courts.

### Middle East, Asia, and the Americas

The UAE, Saudi Arabia, and Qatar are investing heavily in padel infrastructure, often as part of broader sports diversification strategies. The United States, while still in the early stages, is seeing rapid growth in cities like Miami, New York, Los Angeles, and Dallas. Asia represents the next frontier, with Japan, India, and China showing increasing interest.

## The Professional Game

Professional padel is organized primarily through the Premier Padel tour, sanctioned by the FIP and backed by significant broadcast and sponsorship deals. The tour visits cities across Europe, the Middle East, and the Americas, with prize money and prestige growing each season.

On the men's side, Agustin Tapia and Arturo Coello entered the 2026 season as the top-ranked pair, known for their explosive shot-making and dominance on the Premier Padel circuit. Ale Galan, who won the 2025 World Padel Championship in Kuwait alongside Fede Chingotto, remains one of the sport's biggest names. Other stars include Juan Lebron and Paquito Navarro.

In women's padel, Gemma Triay and Delfina Brea topped the 2025 final rankings, with Ariana Sanchez and Paula Josemaria close behind. The women's game is characterized by extraordinary consistency, tactical intelligence, and increasingly powerful shot-making.

## How to Get Started with Padel

Getting into padel requires less investment than most racket sports.

### What You Need

- **A padel racket:** Beginner-friendly rackets cost between 50 and 150 dollars. Round-shaped rackets with a large sweet spot are ideal for newcomers.
- **Padel balls:** Similar to tennis balls but with slightly less pressure. A can of three typically costs a few dollars.
- **Court shoes:** Any flat-soled court shoe or tennis shoe works. Avoid running shoes with thick, curved soles.
- **Comfortable athletic clothing:** No special gear required.

### Finding Courts and Partners

The easiest way to find padel courts near you is through booking apps like Playtomic, which lists available courts and even helps you find playing partners. Many facilities offer "Americano" format sessions -- social round-robin events where you rotate partners and opponents, making it easy to meet other players.

### Taking Lessons

While padel is easy to pick up casually, even one or two lessons will accelerate your progress significantly. A coach can teach you the correct grip, basic serve technique, and how to read the ball off the walls -- skills that are difficult to develop through trial and error alone.

### Join a Community

Padel's social dimension is its greatest asset. Local clubs, social media groups, and organized leagues make it easy to become part of a community. Many cities now have vibrant padel scenes with regular social events, competitive ladders, and casual weekly games.

## The Future of Padel

Padel's trajectory shows no signs of slowing. With over 35 million players, 70,000 courts projected by 2026, and the sport under serious consideration for future Olympic inclusion, padel is transitioning from niche curiosity to global mainstream. The professional tour is maturing, broadcast coverage is expanding, and new markets are opening every year.

For those who have not yet tried padel, there has never been a better time to step onto the court. The sport offers something rare: a genuine combination of physical activity, strategic depth, social connection, and pure fun. Whether you are a lifelong athlete looking for a new challenge or someone who has never picked up a racket, padel has a place for you.
